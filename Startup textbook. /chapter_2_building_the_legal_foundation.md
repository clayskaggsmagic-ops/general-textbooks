# Chapter 2: Building the Legal Foundation

## Everything You Need to Do Before You Raise a Dollar

---

### Why This Chapter Exists

Here is a scenario that plays out every year in the startup world:

Two friends build an app. It takes off. An investor offers them $500,000. They're thrilled. Then the
investor's lawyer asks: "Can I see your incorporation documents, your vesting agreements, your IP
assignment agreements, and your 83(b) election filings?"

The founders stare blankly. They never incorporated. They never discussed equity splits. They never
filed anything. The investor walks away, not because the product was bad, but because investing in a
company with no legal foundation is like buying a house with no deed — it doesn't matter how nice the
house is if nobody can prove who owns it.

This chapter covers everything you need to have in place before an investor will take you seriously.
Some of this will feel tedious. Some of it will feel paranoid. All of it is essential. Skipping any
of it can cost you your company, your friendships, or hundreds of thousands of dollars in taxes.

---

## 2.1 Incorporation: Why Delaware, Why a C-Corp, and Why Right Now

### What Incorporation Actually Is

When you decide to start a company, it doesn't legally exist just because you say it does. A company
is a **legal entity** — a fictional "person" created by filing paperwork with a state government. This
fictional person can own property, enter contracts, sue and be sued, hire employees, and — critically
— issue stock. Until you incorporate, "your company" is just you, personally, doing business. Any
debts are your debts. Any lawsuits are against you personally. Any intellectual property you create is
owned by you as an individual, not by the company.

Incorporation creates a separate legal entity that stands between you and the outside world. This
separation — called the **corporate veil** — is one of the most important concepts in business law.

### Why a C-Corporation (Not an LLC, Not an S-Corp)

There are several types of legal entities you could create:

- **Sole Proprietorship:** Not a separate entity at all. It's just you doing business under a name.
  No liability protection. Never appropriate for a startup.
- **LLC (Limited Liability Company):** A flexible entity that provides liability protection but is
  taxed in weird ways for venture-funded companies. LLCs are great for real estate, consulting firms,
  and small businesses. They are terrible for venture-backed startups.
- **S-Corporation:** A special tax designation that limits you to 100 shareholders, all of whom must
  be U.S. citizens or residents. Venture funds, foreign investors, and most institutional investors
  cannot hold S-Corp stock. Dead on arrival for fundraising.
- **C-Corporation:** The standard entity for venture-backed startups. It can have unlimited
  shareholders, multiple classes of stock (common and preferred), and is the entity type that every
  VC and their lawyers expect to see. If you're raising venture capital, **you are forming a Delaware
  C-Corporation.** End of discussion.

### Why Delaware (Even If You Don't Live There)

This is one of the most counterintuitive things in startup law: your company should be incorporated in
**Delaware,** regardless of where you live, where your office is, or where your customers are.

Why?

1. **Delaware corporate law is the most developed in the country.** The Delaware Court of Chancery has
   been deciding corporate law cases for over 200 years. There is a vast body of case law that
   provides predictability. When a legal question arises about your company's governance, there's
   almost certainly a Delaware precedent. This matters enormously — legal predictability reduces risk
   for everyone.

2. **Every VC lawyer knows Delaware law.** When you raise money, both sides hire lawyers. If your
   company is a Delaware C-Corp, both your lawyer and the investor's lawyer speak the same language.
   If you're a Wyoming LLC or a California S-Corp, everything gets more complicated and more expensive.

3. **Delaware is business-friendly.** Not "business-friendly" in a political sense — in a practical
   sense. Delaware's Division of Corporations is efficient, responsive, and set up to handle millions
   of corporations. Filing amendments, issuing stock, and managing corporate records is smooth.

4. **Venture investors expect it.** This alone is reason enough. If you show up to a VC meeting as a
   Texas LLC, you haven't done anything illegal — but you've signaled that you're new to this and
   haven't gotten good advice. The VC will likely make re-incorporation to a Delaware C-Corp a
   condition of investment, which costs time and money.

### How to Actually Incorporate

The logistics of incorporating are surprisingly simple:

1. **Use a service like Clerky, Stripe Atlas, or a startup lawyer.** Clerky is the gold standard for
   startup incorporation — it's designed specifically for venture-track companies and generates all
   the standard documents (certificate of incorporation, bylaws, board consent, etc.) for a flat fee
   of a few hundred dollars. Stripe Atlas is similar and includes a bank account setup. Or you can
   have a lawyer do it for $2,000–$5,000.

2. **File a Certificate of Incorporation with the Delaware Division of Corporations.** This is the
   founding document. It specifies your company name, the number of authorized shares, the par value
   of those shares, and the registered agent in Delaware.

3. **Adopt bylaws.** Bylaws are the operating rules of your corporation — how the board of directors
   works, how shareholders vote, how meetings are conducted. At the early stage, you'll use
   boilerplate bylaws. They'll get more complex after you raise money.

4. **Issue founder shares.** The corporation authorizes shares in its certificate of incorporation,
   but those shares don't belong to anyone until they're issued. You'll issue shares to each founder
   in exchange for their contributions (IP, cash, labor) at the par value (typically $0.0001 per
   share). More on this below.

5. **Get an EIN (Employer Identification Number).** This is your company's tax ID, issued by the IRS.
   You need it to open a bank account, hire employees, and file taxes. It's free and takes 10 minutes
   online.

6. **Register as a foreign corporation in your home state.** "Foreign" here means foreign to the
   state, not foreign to the country. If you incorporated in Delaware but operate in California,
   you need to register in California as a "foreign corporation doing business" there. This subjects
   you to California taxes and regulations but is legally required.

7. **Open a bank account.** You need a business bank account. Do not commingle personal and business
   funds — this is one of the fastest ways to lose the liability protection that incorporation
   provides ("piercing the corporate veil").

### Common Incorporation Mistakes

**Waiting too long to incorporate.** If you're building a product with a co-founder, incorporate now.
Not "when we raise money." Not "when we have customers." Now. Every day you work on the product
without a corporate entity creates ambiguity about who owns the IP, who owns what percentage, and
what happens if things go sideways.

**Choosing the wrong entity type.** If someone tells you to start as an LLC and "convert later," they
are giving you bad advice for a venture-track startup. Converting an LLC to a C-Corp is possible but
creates tax complications, costs money, and delays your raise. Start as a Delaware C-Corp.

**Issuing too few authorized shares.** Your certificate of incorporation specifies how many shares the
company is authorized to issue. A common mistake is authorizing 1,000 shares or 100,000 shares. The
standard is to authorize **10,000,000 shares** and issue most of them to the founders at $0.0001 per
share (par value). This gives you room to issue shares to employees, advisors, and investors without
needing to amend your certificate of incorporation (which requires a board vote and a filing fee).

**Forgetting to issue shares to founders.** Incorporating the company and issuing shares are separate
steps. Some founders incorporate and then forget to actually issue themselves shares. This creates a
company with no owners, which is a legal mess.

## 2.2 Founder Equity: The Conversation That Makes or Breaks Companies

### The Most Dangerous Conversation You'll Ever Have

How do you split ownership among the founding team? This question has ended more friendships, ruined
more companies, and spawned more lawsuits than any other issue in the startup world. It feels awkward,
and many founders avoid it or resolve it too quickly with a handshake and a vague "50/50 sounds fair."

Let's be direct: **if you avoid this conversation or handle it poorly, it will come back. And when it
comes back, it will be 10x harder and more painful than if you'd dealt with it now.**

### The Equal Split Fallacy

The default instinct is to split equity equally among all founders. Two founders? 50/50. Three
founders? 33/33/33. It feels democratic. It avoids the discomfort of saying "I deserve more."

Equal splits are sometimes the right answer. But often, they're a compromise made to avoid conflict
rather than a reflection of reality. Here are the factors that should inform the split:

1. **Who had the idea?** This matters less than people think. Ideas are cheap; execution is expensive.
   Having the idea might be worth a few percentage points of additional equity, but not a 20% premium.

2. **Who built the prototype?** If one founder spent six months writing code before the other joined,
   that's significant. Time invested before incorporation is real value.

3. **Who's going full-time first?** A founder who quits their job to work on the startup full-time is
   taking a much larger risk than a founder who keeps their day job and contributes evenings and
   weekends. Risk should be compensated.

4. **Who brings critical skills that the other founders don't have?** If one founder is a deep domain
   expert without whom the product can't be built, that expertise has value.

5. **Who's the CEO?** In many startups, one founder is the de facto leader — the person who will
   interface with investors, lead hiring, set strategy, and take ultimate responsibility. This role
   carries significant weight and often merits a larger share (3–10% more).

6. **Who has more opportunity cost?** A founder who's leaving a $300K/year job at Google is giving up
   more (in the short term) than a founder leaving a $60K/year job. This doesn't mean they deserve
   5x the equity, but it's a factor.

### A Framework for the Equity Conversation

Instead of negotiating like adversaries, try this approach:

1. **Acknowledge that this is hard.** Start by saying, "This is an uncomfortable conversation, but
   it's one of the most important we'll ever have. Let's approach it honestly."

2. **Each founder writes down what they think the split should be, independently.** Then share. If
   you're within 5–10% of each other, you're in great shape. Negotiate the gap.

3. **Document the reasoning.** Write down why you arrived at the split. This becomes important later
   if circumstances change or a founder leaves.

4. **Plan for the future.** An equity split is a snapshot of value at the time of founding. What
   happens if one founder's role grows significantly? What if someone stops contributing? Build in
   mechanisms (like vesting — covered next) to handle these scenarios.

### Dynamic Equity Splits and Why to Avoid Them

Some advisors recommend "dynamic equity" models where ownership adjusts based on ongoing contributions
(hours worked, revenue generated, etc.). The most well-known framework is the Slicing Pie model.

These models sound fair in theory but create nightmares in practice:

- **Investors hate them.** VCs want to see a fixed cap table, not a spreadsheet that changes monthly.
  A dynamic equity model signals that the founders couldn't agree on a split, which is a red flag.
- **They create perverse incentives.** If equity is tied to hours, founders are incentivized to log
  hours rather than create value.
- **They're hard to administer.** Tracking contributions fairly is subjective and leads to disputes.

For a venture-track startup, agree on a split, implement vesting, and move on. The split doesn't have
to be perfect — it has to be clear, agreed upon, and documented.

## 2.3 Vesting: The Mechanism That Saves Companies

### What Vesting Is

**Vesting** is the mechanism by which your equity is earned over time. Even though you own your shares
from day one, vesting means those shares are subject to a **repurchase right** by the company if you
leave before they've fully vested.

In plain English: you get your shares on a schedule. If you leave before the schedule is complete, the
company can buy back the unvested shares at the original purchase price (usually $0.0001/share —
essentially free).

### Why Vesting Exists

Vesting solves the most common and most devastating founder problem: **the cofounder who leaves.**

Imagine this scenario without vesting: You and your cofounder split the company 50/50. Six months
later, your cofounder decides startups aren't for them and goes back to their corporate job. Without
vesting, they still own 50% of your company. You're now doing 100% of the work while someone who
contributed for six months owns half the equity. Good luck raising money — no investor will back a
company where a departed founder controls half the cap table.

With vesting: if the cofounder leaves after six months, the company can repurchase most of their
shares. They keep only the portion that vested during their six months. Problem solved.

**Vesting protects you from your cofounder. And it protects your cofounder from you.** Both of you
should want it.

### The Standard Vesting Schedule

The industry standard for founder vesting is **four-year vesting with a one-year cliff:**

- **Four-year total vest.** Your shares vest (become fully yours) over four years.
- **One-year cliff.** No shares vest during the first year. If you leave before the one-year mark,
  you get zero. On your one-year anniversary, 25% of your shares vest all at once.
- **Monthly vesting after the cliff.** After the cliff, shares vest monthly — 1/48th of the total
  per month (or equivalently, 1/36th of the remaining shares per month). So after 18 months, you
  own 25% + (6 × 1/48) = 37.5% of your shares.

**Why the cliff?** The cliff exists to protect against the "tourist founder" — someone who joins,
contributes for a few months, and then leaves. Without a cliff, they'd walk away with a meaningful
chunk of equity. The cliff ensures that someone has to stick around for at least a year to earn any
equity at all.

### Single vs. Double Trigger Acceleration

**Acceleration** is when vesting speeds up — shares vest faster than normal, or all at once. There
are two types:

**Single-trigger acceleration:** All shares vest immediately upon a single event, usually the company
being acquired. This means if BigCo buys your startup, all your unvested shares become vested shares
before the acquisition closes.

- **Pros for founders:** You're fully vested when the company sells, regardless of timing.
- **Cons for everyone else:** The acquirer wants the founders to stay and work post-acquisition.
  Single-trigger removes that incentive. Many VCs and acquirers push back hard against single-trigger.
- **Bottom line:** Single-trigger for founders is fairly rare and generally a bad idea because it makes
  your company harder to acquire.

**Double-trigger acceleration:** Shares accelerate only when two events occur: (1) the company is
acquired AND (2) the founder is terminated or demoted within 12–24 months after the acquisition.

- This protects you from being fired immediately after an acquisition just so the acquirer can reclaim
  your unvested shares. It doesn't give you a free exit — you still have to stay and work unless
  you're unfairly pushed out.
- **Double-trigger is the standard** and is what most investors expect to see.

### What Happens When a Founder Is Already Partially Vested at Fundraise

If you've been working for a year before your first fundraise, you've already earned a year of equity
through sweat. Should that count?

Yes. This is handled through **credit for time served.** If you started your company 12 months ago
and set up standard four-year vesting, you can enter the fundraise with 12 months of vesting credit.
This means 25% of your shares are already vested (past the cliff), and the remaining 75% vest over
the next three years.

Some investors will push back and want you to restart vesting from zero at the time of investment.
This is aggressive. A reasonable compromise is to acknowledge time already served and vest the
remainder on a four-year schedule from incorporation, not from investment.

## 2.4 The 83(b) Election: The 30-Day Tax Time Bomb

### What It Is and Why It's the Most Important Tax Form You'll Ever File

This section is about a single IRS form that, if you miss it, can cost you **hundreds of thousands of
dollars in taxes.** Not hypothetically. Actually. There is no fix, no do-over, and no appeal. This is
the single highest-stakes administrative task in early-stage startup life, and most founders learn about
it too late.

Here's the setup:

When you're issued founder shares that are subject to vesting, the IRS considers those shares to be
"restricted" — you don't fully own them yet because the company can buy them back. Under normal tax
rules, you're taxed when the shares vest (when the restriction lifts), not when they're issued. And
you're taxed on the **fair market value at the time of vesting, minus what you paid for them.**

At the time your shares are issued, the company is worth essentially nothing. Your shares cost $0.0001
each. The "value" of your restricted shares is basically zero. No tax.

But what happens when shares vest over four years, and during those four years, the company raises
money, grows, and the shares increase in value?

**Example without 83(b) election:**

- You're issued 5,000,000 shares at $0.0001/share. Total cost: $500.
- Year 1: 1,250,000 shares vest. The company has raised money; shares are now worth $0.50 each.
  You owe income tax on 1,250,000 × ($0.50 - $0.0001) = $624,875 in ordinary income.
  At a ~37% tax rate, that's **$231,204 in taxes.** And you haven't sold any shares. You have no
  cash. You just owe the IRS $231,204.
- Year 2, 3, 4: Same problem. As the shares vest and the value increases, you owe more taxes each
  year on shares you can't sell.

This is called **phantom income** — taxable income with no corresponding cash. It has literally forced
founders to sell shares at a discount to cover tax bills, or in extreme cases, to leave their own
companies.

### The 83(b) Election: The Fix

Section 83(b) of the Internal Revenue Code allows you to **elect to be taxed at the time the shares
are issued, rather than when they vest.** By filing an 83(b) election, you're telling the IRS: "I want
to pay taxes on these shares right now, when they're worth almost nothing."

**Example with 83(b) election:**

- You're issued 5,000,000 shares at $0.0001/share. Total cost: $500.
- You file an 83(b) election within 30 days.
- The IRS taxes you on the current value minus what you paid: 5,000,000 × ($0.0001 - $0.0001) = $0.
  Your tax is **$0.** (Or perhaps a trivially small amount if the shares are valued slightly above
  par.)
- When the shares vest over four years, there's no additional tax event. You already paid.
- When you eventually sell the shares (exit, IPO, acquisition), you pay long-term capital gains tax
  (currently ~20%) on the difference between your sale price and your purchase price. Long-term
  capital gains rates are significantly lower than ordinary income tax rates.

### The Rules

1. **You must file the 83(b) election within 30 days of being issued the shares.** Not 30 business
   days. Not 30 days from vesting. Not 30 days from when you learned about it. **30 calendar days
   from issuance.** If you miss this deadline, it cannot be extended. There is no exception. The IRS
   does not care that you didn't know about it.

2. **You file it by mailing it to the IRS.** Yes, mailing. Physical mail. In the year 2026, the
   mechanism for protecting yourself from hundreds of thousands of dollars in taxes is putting paper
   in an envelope and mailing it to the IRS service center. Send it via certified mail so you have
   proof of the postmark date. Also keep a copy for yourself and give a copy to the company.

3. **You also need to attach a copy to your personal tax return** for the year in which the shares
   were issued.

4. **If the company's shares are worth more than what you paid, you owe taxes on the difference
   immediately.** At founding, this is usually $0 or close to it. But if you file an 83(b) election
   months after founding, after the company has raised money, the shares may be worth more, and you
   could owe real taxes. This is why you file the 83(b) election immediately — ideally, the same week
   you're issued shares.

### The Downside Risk

The one risk of the 83(b) election: if the company fails and your shares become worthless, you can't
get a refund on the taxes you paid. But since you filed at near-zero value, you probably paid near-zero
taxes. The downside is minimal compared to the upside.

### Practical Steps

1. **The moment founder shares are issued, file the 83(b) election.** Do not wait. Put it on your
   calendar. Make your lawyer remind you. Do it before you do anything else.
2. **Use a template.** Your lawyer or incorporation service (Clerky, etc.) will provide the form. It's
   a one-page document.
3. **Send via certified mail to the IRS.** Keep the certified mail receipt.
4. **Keep a copy in your company's records.**
5. **Give a copy to your accountant** so it's included with your tax return.

If you take away one thing from this entire textbook, let it be this: **file your 83(b) election
within 30 days of receiving founder shares.**

## 2.5 Founder Agreements: The Prenup for Your Business Marriage

### Why You Need a Written Agreement

You and your cofounder might be best friends. You might have unshakeable trust. You might think a
written agreement is unnecessary or even insulting.

You're wrong. Here's why:

1. **People change.** The person you start a company with today may not be the same person in two years.
   Life circumstances change — marriages, children, financial pressures, health issues. Your cofounder
   might discover they hate startup life. You might discover you have fundamentally different visions for
   the company. Without a written agreement, separating is a legal and emotional catastrophe.

2. **Memories differ.** You might remember agreeing that you'd be CEO and your cofounder would be CTO.
   Your cofounder might remember agreeing that you'd share all major decisions equally. Without
   documentation, these disagreements become "he said, she said" fights with no resolution mechanism.

3. **Investors require it.** Any serious investor will ask to see your founder agreements during due
   diligence. If you don't have them, the investor's lawyer will flag it as a risk. Some investors
   will walk away.

### What the Agreement Should Cover

A comprehensive founder agreement (sometimes called a "co-founder agreement" or incorporated into the
company's stockholder agreement) should address:

**Equity split and vesting.** The percentage each founder owns, the vesting schedule, and what happens
to unvested shares when someone leaves.

**Roles and responsibilities.** Who is CEO? Who is CTO? Who makes final decisions on product, hiring,
fundraising, spending? These don't have to be rigid — at an early stage, everyone does everything —
but the decision-making framework should be clear.

**Compensation.** What salaries will founders take? (Often zero or near-zero at the pre-seed stage.)
When does salary kick in? Is it retroactive? What happens if one founder wants to take a salary and
the other doesn't?

**Intellectual property assignment.** All IP created for the company belongs to the company, not to
the individual founder who created it. This seems obvious but must be legally documented. (More on
this in Section 2.7.)

**What happens when a founder leaves.** This is the most important section and the one people are most
reluctant to write. It should cover:
- **Voluntary departure:** The founder quits. Unvested shares are repurchased at the original
  price (essentially confiscated). Vested shares are retained. The departing founder may have
  restrictions on competing or soliciting employees.
- **Termination for cause:** The founder is fired for misconduct, fraud, or gross negligence. This
  typically results in forfeiture of a larger portion of equity (sometimes even vested shares, 
  depending on the agreement and the severity of the cause).
- **Termination without cause:** The founder is asked to leave even though they haven't done anything
  wrong — the team just isn't working. This is the trickiest scenario and should be addressed
  explicitly: What equity do they keep? Is there acceleration? What's the transition plan?
- **Disability or death:** Morbid but necessary. What happens to a founder's shares if they die or
  become permanently disabled? Insurance? Buyback provisions?

**Non-compete and non-solicitation.** Can a departing founder immediately start a competing company?
Can they recruit your employees? These clauses vary in enforceability by state (California generally
doesn't enforce non-competes, for example), but having them in the agreement sets expectations.

**Dispute resolution.** If the founders disagree on something fundamental, what's the process?
Mediation? Arbitration? Voting based on equity ownership? Having a predefined resolution mechanism
prevents disputes from becoming lawsuits.

**Deadlock provisions.** What happens if there are two founders with equal equity and they
fundamentally can't agree? This is called a "deadlock." Some agreements include a "shotgun clause"
(also called a "buy-sell" or "Texas shootout"): one founder offers to buy the other's shares at a
stated price, and the other founder can either accept or turn the tables and buy the first founder's
shares at the same price. It sounds aggressive, but it's an effective way to resolve irreconcilable
differences.

### Co-Founder "Dating"

A brief but important aside: the best way to avoid founder disputes is to **choose your co-founder
carefully in the first place.** Many experienced founders recommend a period of "co-founder dating"
— working together on a project for 3–6 months before formally committing to a company together.
During this period, you'll learn:

- How they handle disagreements
- Whether they work at the same intensity as you
- Whether your skills and temperaments complement each other
- Whether you fundamentally agree on what you're building and why

## 2.6 Your First Lawyer: Why You Need One and How to Choose One

### The Non-Negotiable Hire

Many technical founders think of lawyers as an unnecessary expense. They'll incorporate using an online
service (fine), skip the founder agreement (not fine), and plan to "get a lawyer when we need one"
(dangerous).

You need a startup lawyer from the beginning. Not a general practice attorney who also does divorces
and real estate closings. A **startup and venture capital attorney** at a firm that works with
early-stage companies.

Why? Because:

1. **Incorporation documents need to be done right.** Your certificate of incorporation, bylaws,
   restricted stock purchase agreements, and 83(b) elections all need to be correct from day one.
   Fixing them later is expensive and sometimes impossible.

2. **A good startup lawyer has done this hundreds of times.** They know what investors will ask for,
   what terms are standard, and where founders commonly get burned. Their advice prevents problems.

3. **They'll do early work for deferred payment or reduced fees.** Many top startup law firms offer
   deferred fee arrangements for early-stage startups. They'll do your incorporation and early legal
   work for a few thousand dollars or even defer payment until you raise money. They're investing in
   the relationship — if you grow, you'll need them for your fundraise, and that's where they make
   real money. This is industry standard. If a law firm doesn't offer it, find one that does.

4. **They protect you in the fundraise.** When an investor sends you a term sheet, you need someone
   who can tell you what's standard, what's aggressive, and what's a deal-breaker. You cannot do this
   yourself. You don't know what "1x non-participating liquidation preference" means yet, and that's
   okay — your lawyer does.

### How to Choose a Startup Lawyer

**Look for firms that specialize in startup and venture capital law.** A short list of well-known firms
in this space: Goodwin Procter, Wilson Sonsini, Cooley, Gunderson Dettmer, Fenwick & West, Orrick.
There are many others, including excellent small firms, solo practitioners, and regional firms that
focus on startups.

**Ask other founders.** The best referral for a startup lawyer comes from a founder who has worked with
them through a fundraise. Ask in your network, your accelerator cohort, or online founder communities.

**Look for a partner or associate who works with companies at your stage.** Large firms have lawyers
who focus on Series B and later; you want someone who regularly works with pre-seed and seed startups.
They'll be more responsive to your (smaller) legal needs and more attuned to the issues you face.

**Negotiate the engagement.** Don't accept the first fee proposal. Ask about deferred payment,
startup packages, and fixed-fee services for incorporation. This is expected and does not make you
look cheap — it makes you look like someone who understands how the startup legal market works.

## 2.7 Intellectual Property: The Silent Killer

### Why IP Matters More Than You Think

Your startup's most valuable asset is probably its intellectual property: the code you've written,
the algorithms you've developed, the designs you've created, the brand you're building. If the
ownership of that IP is unclear or disputed, your company may be worthless — regardless of how good
the technology is.

Investors know this. IP issues are one of the top reasons deals fall apart during due diligence.

### The IP Assignment Problem

Here is a scenario that happens constantly:

Before incorporating, Alice and Bob spent six months building a prototype. Alice wrote the backend;
Bob designed the frontend. They never signed any documents. Then they incorporate and raise money.

Who owns the code? **Alice and Bob do, personally.** Not the company. The company didn't exist when
the code was written, so the company can't own it. Alice and Bob are just two people who wrote some
software. The company has no IP.

To fix this, Alice and Bob need to **assign** their IP to the company — meaning they formally transfer
ownership of everything they've created to the corporate entity. This is done through a document
called an **IP Assignment Agreement** (sometimes called a **Proprietary Information and Inventions
Assignment Agreement** or **PIIA**). Every founder must sign one. There are no exceptions.

### What an IP Assignment Agreement Covers

- **All prior work.** Any code, designs, algorithms, or other IP created before incorporation that
  will be used in the company's products must be explicitly assigned to the company. The agreement
  should list these works on a schedule. Anything not listed is not assigned.

- **All future work.** Anything created by the founder during their work for the company belongs to
  the company. This is standard employment doctrine for employees, but founders need to agree to it
  explicitly.

- **Confidentiality.** The founder agrees not to disclose the company's confidential information —
  trade secrets, customer lists, business strategies, etc.

- **Non-use of third-party IP.** The founder represents that they haven't used anyone else's
  proprietary code or materials in the company's products. This protects the company from a scenario
  where your cofounder copied code from their previous employer, and that employer sues for IP theft.

### The Prior Employer Problem

This is a particularly nasty issue. If a founder previously worked at a tech company, that company
almost certainly had an IP assignment agreement that covers anything the employee created during their
employment — and sometimes things created outside of work if they relate to the employer's business.

This means:

- If your cofounder built part of your prototype while still employed at Google, Google may have a
  claim on that code.
- If your cofounder used knowledge or techniques they learned at their previous job, the previous
  employer might argue that the startup's IP is derivative of their own.
- Even if no one is actually claiming infringement, an investor's lawyer will ask: "Did any founder
  create IP for this company while employed elsewhere?" If the answer is yes (or unclear), it's a
  due diligence problem.

**The solution:** Every founder should review their previous employment agreements, understand what
they assigned to previous employers, and ensure that the startup's IP is genuinely new and
independently created. If there's any ambiguity, disclose it to your lawyer immediately.

### Open Source and Third-Party Libraries

Your product almost certainly uses open-source software. Open-source licenses vary dramatically in
their requirements:

- **Permissive licenses (MIT, BSD, Apache 2.0):** These are generally safe. You can use, modify, and
  distribute the software with minimal restrictions. You need to include the original copyright
  notice, but that's about it.

- **Copyleft licenses (GPL, AGPL):** These are significantly more restrictive. If you use GPL-licensed
  code in your product and distribute that product, you may be required to release your own source
  code under the GPL as well. The AGPL extends this requirement even to software offered as a
  service (SaaS). Using AGPL code in a closed-source commercial product is a legal and business risk
  that investors will flag.

- **Proprietary third-party software:** If you're using APIs, SDKs, or libraries from commercial
  vendors, check the license terms. Some restrict commercial use or require attribution.

Before raising money, **audit your open-source dependencies.** Know what licenses you're using. If
you have GPL or AGPL dependencies, be prepared to explain your legal theory for why this is or isn't
a problem. Ideally, replace them with permissive alternatives.

### Patents

Patents protect inventions — novel, non-obvious methods or systems. For most early-stage startups,
patents are **not a priority.** They're expensive ($10,000–$30,000 per patent), take years to issue,
and are difficult to enforce. A few exceptions:

- **Deep tech startups** (biotech, hardware, advanced materials) often need patents because their
  competitive advantage is a specific invention that competitors could replicate.
- **Startups in patent-heavy industries** (medical devices, telecommunications) may need patents for
  freedom to operate — meaning that without their own patent portfolio, they're vulnerable to
  infringement claims from incumbents.
- **Defensive patents:** Some companies file patents not to enforce them but to prevent others from
  patenting the same thing and suing them.

For most software startups at the pre-seed or seed stage, your competitive advantage is speed of
execution and product quality, not patents. Don't spend your limited cash on patent filings unless
you have a specific strategic reason.

If you do pursue patents, file a **provisional patent application** first. Provisionals cost
$1,000–$3,000, establish a priority date (important if someone else files a similar patent later),
and give you 12 months to decide whether to file a full utility patent. They are a cheap way to
preserve your rights while you figure out if patenting makes strategic sense.

### Trade Secrets

Not everything should be patented. Some things are better protected as **trade secrets** — information
that derives economic value from being secret. Google's search algorithm is a trade secret. Coca-Cola's
formula is a trade secret.

Trade secret protection requires:
- The information must actually be kept secret (limited access, NDAs, security measures)
- The information must derive value from being secret
- The company must take reasonable steps to maintain its secrecy

Unlike patents, trade secrets don't expire. But if someone independently discovers the same
information, or if you fail to keep it secret, you lose protection.

## 2.8 Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS): A Tax Windfall You Might Not Know About

### What QSBS Is

This is one of the most valuable and least known tax provisions for startup founders. Under Section
1202 of the Internal Revenue Code, if you hold **Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS)** for more
than five years, you can exclude up to **$10 million** (or 10x your cost basis, whichever is greater)
of capital gains from federal income tax when you sell the stock.

Read that again: **if your startup succeeds and you sell your shares, you might owe $0 in federal
capital gains tax on the first $10 million of gains.** For a married couple filing jointly, this
exclusion can apply separately to each spouse's shares, potentially excluding $20 million.

### Requirements for QSBS Treatment

For stock to qualify as QSBS:

1. **The company must be a C-Corporation.** (Another reason to be a C-Corp, not an LLC.)
2. **The company must have aggregate gross assets of $50 million or less** at the time the stock is
   issued and immediately after. (This means you qualify at founding and at the seed stage; you might
   not qualify by Series C.)
3. **The stock must be acquired at original issuance** — meaning you got it directly from the company,
   not by buying it from another shareholder.
4. **You must hold the stock for more than five years.**
5. **The company must use at least 80% of its assets in an active trade or business.** (Not holding
   real estate or investments.)
6. **Certain industries are excluded:** hospitality, banking, insurance, farming, mining, and
   professional services (law, accounting, medicine). Technology companies generally qualify.

### Why This Matters for Founders

If you start a company, issue yourself shares at or near founding when the company is worth nothing,
and hold those shares for more than five years before an exit, you could pay zero federal capital gains
tax on up to $10 million in gains. This is not a loophole — it's a deliberate incentive in the tax
code to encourage people to start and invest in small businesses.

**Practical implication:** This is another reason to incorporate as a C-Corp, issue shares early, and
file your 83(b) election. The five-year clock starts when you receive the shares, and the 83(b)
election ensures you're taxed at the low founding-stage value rather than the higher vested-stage value.

### State-Level Variations

Some states conform to the federal QSBS exclusion. Others don't. California, notably, **does not**
recognize the QSBS exclusion — if you're a California resident, you'll still owe California state
capital gains tax even if you're federally exempt. Check your state's treatment with your accountant.

## 2.9 Your Cap Table: The Spreadsheet of Power

### What a Cap Table Is

A **capitalization table** (cap table) is the document that shows who owns what in your company. At
the simplest level, it's a spreadsheet listing:

- Every shareholder's name
- The number of shares they own
- The type of shares (common, preferred, options, SAFEs)
- The percentage of total ownership each person represents

At founding, your cap table is simple:

| Shareholder | Shares     | Type    | % Ownership |
|-------------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Alice       | 5,000,000  | Common  | 50%         |
| Bob         | 5,000,000  | Common  | 50%         |
| **Total**   | 10,000,000 | —       | 100%        |

After a fundraise with an option pool, it might look like:

| Shareholder   | Shares     | Type      | % Ownership |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Alice         | 5,000,000  | Common    | 40%         |
| Bob           | 5,000,000  | Common    | 40%         |
| Investor Fund | 1,250,000  | Preferred | 10%         |
| ESOP (unissued)| 1,250,000 | Reserved  | 10%         |
| **Total**     | 12,500,000 | —         | 100%        |

### Why the Cap Table Matters

Your cap table determines:
- **Who controls decisions.** Voting rights are usually proportional to share ownership.
- **Who gets paid in an exit.** The cap table (combined with the legal terms attached to each class
  of shares) determines the payout waterfall.
- **How attractive you are to investors.** A clean cap table with reasonable founder ownership is
  attractive. A messy cap table with too many small shareholders, unclear ownership, or excessive
  dilution is a red flag.

### Cap Table Hygiene

**Keep it clean from day one.** Use a cap table management tool like Carta, Pulley, or AngelList Stack.
These tools are purpose-built for managing startup equity, tracking vesting, issuing stock options, and
generating reports for investors and tax purposes. Do not manage your cap table in a personal Excel
spreadsheet — you'll make mistakes, and those mistakes will haunt you during due diligence.

**Avoid excessive fragmentation.** Having 50 tiny angel investors who each own 0.1% creates an
administrative nightmare. Every corporate action (amending bylaws, authorizing new shares, selling the
company) may require shareholder consent, and getting 50 people to sign documents is expensive and
time-consuming. If you're taking small angel checks, batch them through a SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle)
— a single entity that pools multiple investors' money and appears as one line on your cap table.

**Watch for dead equity.** Dead equity is ownership held by people who no longer contribute to the
company — departed founders, early advisors who stopped advising, or friends-and-family investors who
hold a large stake from a tiny early check. Dead equity discourages future investors and employees,
because every share held by a non-contributor is a share that isn't motivating someone who's actually
doing the work.

## 2.10 State Registrations, Taxes, and Boring-But-Essential Compliance

### What You Can't Ignore

After incorporating in Delaware, you'll need to deal with the regulatory and tax obligations of
actually running a business. This isn't exciting, but skipping it creates serious problems down the
road.

**Delaware Franchise Tax.** Delaware charges an annual franchise tax to maintain your corporation's
good standing. For startups, this is typically $400–$500/year using the "Authorized Shares" method.
There is also an annual report fee of $50. If you don't pay these, Delaware can void your corporation,
which is exactly as bad as it sounds.

**Foreign qualification.** If you operate in a state other than Delaware (which you almost certainly
do, since you probably don't have an office in Delaware), you need to register as a "foreign
corporation" in each state where you have employees or a physical presence. This typically costs
$100–$800 per state and subjects you to that state's taxes and employment laws.

**Federal taxes.** Your C-Corp files a separate federal tax return (Form 1120). At the early stage,
you probably have no revenue and are operating at a loss. These losses can be carried forward to
offset future income (the **Net Operating Loss** or **NOL** carryforward). Your accountant handles
this, but know that it exists — your early losses have future tax value.

**Payroll taxes and employment law.** The moment you have employees (including yourself, once you
start drawing a salary), you need to withhold federal and state income taxes, Social Security, and
Medicare from paychecks. You also need to pay employer-side payroll taxes. Use a payroll provider
(Gusto, Rippling, Justworks, etc.) — do not try to do this manually.

**Sales tax.** If you sell a product (especially a physical good or, increasingly, SaaS), you may need
to collect and remit sales tax. Sales tax compliance in the U.S. is notoriously complex because each
state has different rules. If this applies to you, use a service like Avalara or TaxJar.

### The "We'll Deal With It Later" Trap

Many founders skip these compliance steps, planning to "clean it up later." Here's what "later" looks
like: an investor is ready to wire you $2 million. Their lawyer does due diligence and discovers that:
- Your Delaware franchise tax is two years overdue.
- You never registered as a foreign corporation in California, where all three of your employees live.
- You have no payroll provider and have been paying employees as 1099 contractors (which is illegal
  if they're actually employees).

The investor doesn't walk away — but they make you fix all of this before they'll close. This adds
4–8 weeks to your fundraise, costs $10,000–$30,000 in legal and back-tax fees, and makes you look
disorganized. Do it right from the start.

## 2.11 Advisors: How to Get Help Without Giving Away the Store

### What Startup Advisors Do

Advisors are experienced people who provide guidance, introductions, and credibility in exchange for
a small amount of equity. Great advisors can be transformative — an advisor who's built and sold a
company in your space can save you years of learning from mistakes by pointing them out in advance.

### Standard Advisor Equity

The typical advisor grant is **0.1% to 1.0% of the company,** depending on:

- How much value they'll add (strategic introductions vs. occasional advice)
- How much time they'll commit (one meeting per month vs. hands-on engagement)
- How established the company is (earlier = more equity; later = less)
- How prominent the advisor is

The industry standard framework is the **Founder Institute's FAST Agreement** (Founder/Advisor
Standard Template), which recommends:

| Advisor Level | Commitment          | Typical Equity |
|---------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Standard      | Meeting/month       | 0.25%          |
| Strategic     | Regular involvement | 0.5%           |
| Expert        | Hands-on, deep      | 1.0%           |

Advisor shares should vest — typically over **two years** with a monthly vest and no cliff (or a
three-month cliff). This protects you if the advisor disappears after a few months.

### Common Advisor Mistakes

**Giving too much equity too early.** A first-time founder who gives 3% to an advisor "because they
were really helpful" will regret it when that advisor fades away and owns more of the company than
an engineer who works 60 hours a week.

**Not defining expectations.** An advisor who thinks they'll meet you for coffee once a quarter and an
advisor who commits to weekly calls are providing very different levels of value. Put the expectations
in writing.

**Collecting advisors like Pokémon.** Having 10 advisors each getting 0.5% means you've given away 5%
of your company to people who may or may not actually help. Be selective. Two great advisors are
better than ten mediocre ones.

**Not having a formal agreement.** Advisor relationships should be documented in a written agreement
that specifies the equity amount, vesting schedule, expected commitment, and confidentiality
obligations.

## 2.12 Chapter Summary

This chapter covered the foundational legal and structural work that must be in place before you raise

| Issue founder shares | At incorporation | Yes |

# Chapter 4: The Money People

## How Every Type of Investor Actually Works, What They Want, and Why It Matters

---

### Why You Need to Understand Investors, Not Just Their Money

Most first-time founders think about fundraising from one side: *their* side. How much do I need? What
valuation do I want? When should I raise? These are the right questions to start with, but they're
only half the picture.

The other half — the half that determines whether you get funded, by whom, and on what terms — is
understanding the investor's perspective. Every investor who sits across from you is operating within
a system of incentives, constraints, and obligations that shapes how they evaluate your company, what
terms they'll accept, and how they'll behave as shareholders.

This chapter peels back the curtain. By the end, you'll understand the internal mechanics of a venture
capital fund, the motivations of an angel investor, the hidden priorities of an accelerator, and why
a corporate investor might offer you money that you should think twice about accepting. This knowledge
gives you a negotiating edge that most first-time founders don't have.

---

## 4.1 Venture Capital Firms: The Machine Behind the Check

### How a VC Fund Is Structured

A venture capital firm is not a single entity. It's typically a collection of **funds** — separate
pools of money raised from outside investors (called **Limited Partners** or **LPs**). Each fund has
a fixed life span (typically 10–12 years), a fixed amount of capital, and a specific investment thesis.

Here's the hierarchy:

**The Management Company (the firm itself).** This is the business entity that runs everything — it
has the brand name, website, office, staff, and reputation. Think "Sequoia Capital," "Andreessen
Horowitz," or "Benchmark." The management company doesn't invest money; it manages fund entities that
invest money.

**The Fund(s).** Each fund is a separate legal entity (usually a limited partnership). A firm might
manage multiple funds simultaneously:
- Fund I ($50M, raised in 2018, now largely deployed)
- Fund II ($150M, raised in 2021, actively investing)
- Fund III ($300M, raised in 2024, just getting started)

Each fund has its own pool of capital, its own investments, and its own returns. When a VC says "we
just raised a new fund," they mean they've convinced LPs to commit capital to a new pool.

**General Partners (GPs).** The people who run the fund. GPs make investment decisions, sit on boards,
and manage the portfolio. They are the senior partners at the firm — the names you see in press
releases. GPs have "carried interest" (see below) — their personal financial incentive to pick
winning investments.

**Partners (various titles).** VC firms have a variety of partner-level titles:
- **Managing Partner / Founding Partner:** The firm's founders and most senior decision-makers.
- **General Partner:** A senior investor with full decision-making authority and carry.
- **Partner:** Sometimes equivalent to GP, sometimes a step below.
- **Venture Partner:** A part-time partner, usually a successful operator or founder who invests some
  of their time (and sometimes their own money through the fund). They may or may not have carry.
- **Principal / VP:** A senior non-partner professional. Usually identifies and evaluates deals but
  doesn't have final decision-making authority. They need GP approval to make investments.
- **Associate / Analyst:** Junior team members who source deals, do research, and assist with
  diligence. They don't make investment decisions. Meeting with an associate is usually a screening
  conversation — not a decision-making conversation.

**Why the hierarchy matters to you:** When you take a meeting with "someone at [Big VC Firm]," it
matters enormously whether that person is a GP who can write a check or an associate who's doing
intake. Be respectful to everyone, but understand that your champion inside the firm needs to be
someone who can get a deal done — typically a GP or a senior partner with carry and decision-making
power.

### How LPs and GPs Work Together

**Limited Partners (LPs)** are the people and institutions who provide the money that VCs invest. LPs
include:

- **University endowments** (Harvard, Yale, Stanford — their endowment funds allocate to VC)
- **Pension funds** (CalPERS, teachers' pensions, state retirement systems)
- **Foundations** (Ford Foundation, Melinda French Gates Foundation)
- **Family offices** (wealthy families who invest across asset classes)
- **Sovereign wealth funds** (government-controlled investment funds — Singapore's GIC, Abu Dhabi's
  Mubadala, Norway's Government Pension Fund)
- **Fund of funds** (firms that invest in VC funds rather than startups directly)
- **Corporations** (Google, Microsoft, Intel investing in VC funds, not just directly in startups)
- **High-net-worth individuals** (wealthy people who allocate a portion of assets to VC)

LPs commit capital to the fund but don't make investment decisions. They trust the GPs to pick good
companies. In exchange, the GPs charge two types of fees:

**Management fee.** Typically 2% of the fund's total capital, per year. For a $200M fund, that's
$4M/year — this pays for salaries, office space, travel, and operations. This fee is charged
regardless of performance. Even if every investment goes to zero, the GPs still collect management
fees. (This is why some critics argue that the VC model has a built-in moral hazard.)

**Carried interest (carry).** The GPs' share of the profits. Typically 20% of any gains above the
original invested capital (sometimes above a "hurdle rate" — a minimum return threshold). Carry is
where GPs make real money.

Example: A $200M fund returns $600M (3x). Profits are $400M. The GPs get 20% of $400M = $80M in
carry, split among the GP team. The LPs get the remaining $320M (plus their original $200M back).

**Why this matters to you as a founder:**

1. **GPs are personally incentivized by carry, not management fees.** Carry comes from big winners.
   This is why VCs are looking for outlier returns, not modest ones. A company that returns 5x the
   investment is fine, but the GP is personally searching for the 50x or 100x outcome that generates
   career-defining carry.

2. **Fund size determines check size and return expectations.** A $50M fund writing $1M checks needs
   different outcomes than a $500M fund writing $10M checks. The larger the fund, the larger the
   exit needs to be to move the needle. This is why some companies get rejected by large funds — not
   because the company is bad, but because even a great outcome wouldn't meaningfully impact a
   multi-billion-dollar fund's returns.

3. **Fund vintage matters.** A fund in its early years (Year 1–3) is actively deploying capital and
   looking for new investments. A fund in its later years (Year 7–10) is focused on managing existing
   investments and returning capital to LPs. If you're pitching a firm, find out which fund they'd
   invest from and where that fund is in its lifecycle.

### How Investment Decisions Are Made Inside a VC Firm

This is the part that frustrates founders the most: the black box of VC decision-making. Here's how
it typically works:

**1. Sourcing.** A partner, principal, or associate identifies your company — through a warm
introduction, a demo day, inbound outreach, or a cold pitch (rare but not unheard of). The person who
identifies you becomes your **internal champion** — the person who will advocate for your deal inside
the firm.

**2. First meeting.** You pitch the champion. This is usually 30–60 minutes. They're evaluating: team,
market, product, traction, and whether this fits their thesis. If they're interested, they'll ask for
follow-up materials (deck, data, references).

**3. Due diligence and deeper dives.** The champion does preliminary diligence: calls your references,
reviews your data more closely, talks to people in your industry. They may bring in other partners
for a second meeting to get more perspectives.

**4. Partner meeting.** This is the pivotal step. Your champion presents your company to the full
partnership at a weekly partner meeting. These meetings are typically 2–4 hours, covering multiple
potential deals. The champion presents the opportunity, shares their conviction, and fields questions
and skepticism from other partners.

Different firms have different decision-making processes:
- **Full consensus (Benchmark model):** All partners must agree to invest. One dissenting vote kills
  the deal. This leads to fewer, higher-conviction bets.
- **Champion-based (a16z, Sequoia):** The deal champion has significant latitude to push through
  deals they believe in, though partnership support is still important.
- **Voting (common at many firms):** A majority vote or super-majority determines whether to invest.

**5. Term sheet.** If the partnership approves, you receive a term sheet. This is a huge milestone —
but remember, it's non-binding. The deal can still fall apart during legal due diligence.

**6. Closing.** Legal docs are drafted, due diligence is completed, and money is wired.

**Why this matters to you:** Your main risk is losing your internal champion. If the partner who
loves your deal gets overruled by their partnership, it's done. You can't appeal. You also have no
visibility into the internal dynamics — you might be competing against three other deals the
partnership is evaluating that week. This is why creating urgency matters: if you have a competing
term sheet, your champion can argue that the firm needs to move quickly or lose the deal.

### Reserves and Follow-On Strategy

VC funds don't invest all their capital in first checks. A typical deployment strategy:

- **50–60% of the fund** goes to initial investments (first checks into new portfolio companies)
- **40–50% of the fund** is reserved for **follow-on investments** (investing more in existing
  portfolio companies in later rounds)

This means a $200M fund might make initial investments totaling $100–120M and reserve $80–100M for
follow-ons.

**Why this matters to you:** Your Series A investor will likely participate in your Series B (using
their reserve capital). A fund that has run out of reserves — either because they over-invested early
or because they're supporting too many struggling portfolio companies — may not be able to follow on.
This can create a signaling problem: other investors might wonder why your existing investor isn't
participating. Ask your prospective investor about their reserve strategy before closing.

## 4.2 Angel Investors: Individuals Writing Personal Checks

### Who Angels Are

Angel investors are individuals who invest their own money (not a fund's money) into startups. They
come in many varieties:

**Former founders.** People who've started and sold companies and now invest some of their proceeds in
the next generation of startups. These are often the best angels because they've been in your shoes
and can provide tactical advice alongside capital.

**Tech executives.** Senior employees at big tech companies (VP-level and above at Google, Meta,
Amazon, etc.) who have accumulated significant wealth through equity compensation. They often invest
in areas adjacent to their professional expertise.

**Professional angels.** People who treat angel investing as a primary activity. They may have been
successful in previous careers (finance, medicine, law, real estate) and now deploy capital across
many startups. Professional angels often invest through a structured approach — fixed check sizes,
specific sectors, defined evaluation criteria.

**"Super angels."** Angels who invest at a scale closer to small VC funds — writing $250K–$1M checks
and leading rounds. Jason Calacanis, Elad Gil, and Naval Ravikant are examples of super angels who
have built reputations comparable to institutional VCs.

### How Angels Invest

Angels typically:
- Write checks of $5K–$250K (with super angels going higher)
- Invest in SAFEs or convertible notes (rarely lead priced rounds)
- Make decisions quickly (days, not weeks) — there's no partnership to convince
- Invest based heavily on the founder, their personal knowledge of the market, and "gut feel"
- Provide varying levels of post-investment support, from deeply involved to completely passive

### What Angels Want

Angels have diverse motivations:

1. **Financial return.** Most angels are investing to make money. They understand it's high risk, but
   they expect a portfolio approach to work out: invest in 20+ companies, hope that 1–2 produce 20–50x
   returns to cover the losses elsewhere.

2. **Pattern recognition and excitement.** Many angels invest because they enjoy being close to
   innovation. They want to see new technologies, meet smart founders, and feel connected to the
   startup ecosystem.

3. **Mentorship and legacy.** Former founders often invest because they want to give back — to help
   the next generation avoid the mistakes they made.

4. **Deal flow for a future fund.** Some angels are quietly building a track record so they can raise
   their own VC fund later. They need demonstrated angel returns to pitch to LPs.

5. **Strategic value.** Some angels invest in companies that could be strategically relevant to their
   own interests — a tech executive investing in companies that might be acquired by their employer, or
   a retail operator investing in retail-tech startups whose products they'd be the first customer for.

### The Good and the Bad of Angel Money

**Advantages:**
- Fast decisions (one person decides)
- Personal relationship (they're invested in you, not a fund thesis)
- Flexible terms (usually accept standard SAFEs without negotiation)
- Potential for strong mentorship and tactical advice
- Valuable introductions to customers, partners, and future investors

**Disadvantages:**
- Small check sizes (you might need 10–30 angels to fill a round)
- Variable quality (some are deeply helpful; others write a check and disappear; some are actively
  destructive — micromanaging, overstepping, or providing bad advice with high confidence)
- No institutional support (no back-office team, no recruiting platform, no PR machine)
- Potential for cap table fragmentation (too many small stakeholders)
- Emotional attachment (some angels take company setbacks personally and can become difficult)

### How to Evaluate an Angel

Before accepting angel money, ask:

1. **What's your experience in our market?** An angel who's built a company in your space is 10x
   more valuable than one who's investing based on general interest.
2. **How many other companies have you invested in?** An angel who's made 50 investments understands
   the game. A first-time angel may have unrealistic expectations.
3. **What role do you expect to play post-investment?** Set expectations up front. Monthly coffee?
   Board observer? No contact at all?
4. **Can you introduce me to later-stage investors?** An angel's network is part of their value. An
   angel who can get you meetings with Tier 1 VCs is enormously valuable.
5. **What happens if the company struggles?** Will they be supportive and patient, or will they panic
   and create drama?

## 4.3 Accelerators: The On-Ramp to the Ecosystem

### What Accelerators Are

Accelerators are programs that provide a small amount of capital, mentorship, and community to
early-stage startups in exchange for equity. They run in cohorts — a batch of 10–100 companies goes
through the program together over 3–4 months, culminating in a "demo day" where companies pitch to
a room full of investors.

### The Major Accelerators

**Y Combinator (YC):** The gold standard. YC invests $500K on standard terms ($125K on a SAFE + $375K
on an uncapped MFN SAFE) and takes approximately 7% of the company. YC alumni include Airbnb, Stripe,
DoorDash, Coinbase, and thousands of others. Getting into YC is itself a massive signal — many
investors will take a meeting with any YC company, no questions asked.

YC's real value is the **network.** The alumni network is vast, active, and intensely loyal. YC alumni
help each other with hiring, fundraising, customer introductions, and navigating crises. The "YC
mafia" is a real thing.

**Techstars:** The second-most prominent global accelerator, operating programs in multiple cities.
Techstars typically invests $120K for 6% equity. Programs run for 3 months and are mentor-driven.

**500 Global (formerly 500 Startups):** A large, global accelerator network that invests $150K for
5–6% equity. Known for strong marketing and growth expertise.

**Niche accelerators:** There are hundreds of accelerators focused on specific sectors: healthcare
(Rock Health, Cedars-Sinai Accelerator), fintech (Barclays Accelerator), defense (MD5, AFWERX),
climate (Greentown Labs), and many more. These can be valuable for domain-specific connections and
credibility.

### Is an Accelerator Right for You?

**Yes if:**
- You're a first-time founder and need structured mentorship
- You don't have an existing investor network
- You'd benefit from the cohort community and peer support
- You're applying to a top-tier program (YC, Techstars, 500 Global) where the brand name has material
  value
- You need the capital (even if small) to get started

**No if:**
- You already have traction and investor interest
- The equity cost (5–7%) is too high for what you'd receive
- You'd have to relocate or commit 3 months to a program that doesn't align with your needs
- The accelerator is low-quality — there are many "accelerators" that provide little value and charge
  high equity or fees

### Accelerator Red Flags

- **High equity for low value.** Any program taking more than 7% should provide extraordinary value.
- **Pay-to-play models.** Legitimate accelerators invest in you. If they're charging you tuition or
  fees, it's a class, not an accelerator.
- **No investor network.** The primary value of an accelerator is introductions to investors at demo
  day. If the demo day audience is sparse or low-quality, the program isn't serving its purpose.
- **No successful alumni.** Ask for references from companies in previous cohorts. If no one has gone
  on to raise meaningful follow-on funding, the accelerator isn't working.

## 4.4 Family Offices: The Quiet Money

### What Family Offices Are

A **family office** is a private wealth management firm that serves a single ultra-wealthy family. The
family might be worth $100M to $10B+, and the family office manages all of their investments, estate
planning, tax strategy, and philanthropy. Some family offices allocate a portion of their assets to
venture capital — either by investing directly in startups or by investing in VC funds as LPs.

### Why Family Offices Are Different

Family offices have characteristics that make them fundamentally different from VC funds:

**No fund lifecycle.** A VC fund has a 10-year life. It must return capital within that period. A family
office has a theoretically infinite time horizon. The family's wealth persists across generations.
This means family office investors may be more patient, more willing to hold investments for extended
periods, and less focused on quick exits.

**No LP pressure.** A VC GP must show returns to their LPs. A family office is accountable only to the
family members. This can mean more flexible terms, less aggressive governance, and fewer demands for
board seats.

**Diverse motivations.** The family's patriarch might be investing in your startup because they're
genuinely excited about the technology, because their daughter works in your industry, or because they
want "innovation exposure" in their portfolio. These motivations are less predictable than a VC's
financial calculus.

**Variable sophistication.** Some family offices have professional investment teams with deep venture
experience. Others are managed by the family patriarch's college buddy or the family's longtime
accountant. The quality of support, the speed of decision-making, and the reasonableness of terms
vary enormously.

### Pros and Cons of Family Office Money

**Pros:**
- Patient capital (no 10-year fund clock)
- Potentially large check sizes ($500K–$10M)
- Fewer governance demands
- The family's operational connections (if the family made their wealth in a relevant industry)

**Cons:**
- Slow decision-making (some family offices take months to make a $500K decision)
- Lack of startup expertise (they may not understand your business or provide useful advice)
- Potential for unusual demands (the patriarch wants quarterly in-person meetings, or the family
  wants discounts on your product, or they have strong opinions about your team)
- Signaling risk (in some cases, having a family office lead your round rather than a known VC can
  make it harder to raise from institutional VCs later — they may wonder why "real" VCs didn't invest)

## 4.5 Corporate Venture Capital (CVC): The Strategic Investor

### What CVCs Are

**Corporate Venture Capital** arms are investment divisions within large corporations. Google Ventures
(GV), Intel Capital, Salesforce Ventures, Microsoft's M12, Samsung NEXT, and Qualcomm Ventures are
examples. They invest corporate money (not a separate fund's money) into startups.

### How CVCs Differ from Traditional VCs

CVCs have a dual mandate: **financial returns AND strategic value.**

The financial return part is similar to traditional VC — they want portfolio companies to appreciate
in value. But the strategic part is unique: the parent corporation wants to invest in companies that
are relevant to their business. This might mean:

- **Technology scouting:** Investing in startups that are developing technology the parent corp might
  want to acquire or license.
- **Ecosystem building:** Investing in companies that build on the parent corp's platform (e.g.,
  Salesforce Ventures investing in companies that integrate with Salesforce).
- **Market intelligence:** Getting a window into emerging technologies and market trends.
- **Potential acquisition targets:** CVCs sometimes serve as a "dating period" — invest, get to know
  the company, and potentially acquire them later.

### The CVC Trap: Why Strategic Money Can Be Dangerous

Corporate investors can be extremely valuable — deep pockets, technical resources, distribution
channels, credibility in the market. But they come with hidden risks:

**1. Signaling to competitors.** If Google Ventures invests in your company, your competitors see that
you're in Google's orbit. This may help (Google's stamp of approval) or hurt (other big companies
might not want to work with a Google-adjacent startup, or they might accelerate their own competitive
efforts).

**2. Acquisition soft pressure.** A corporate investor may have invested partly because they want to
eventually acquire you. This creates a tension: do they support you becoming an independent, massive
company (which might make you too expensive to acquire), or do they prefer you stay small enough to
be acquirable? This conflict of interest is subtle but real.

**3. Information leakage.** Your corporate investor has access to your board materials, financial
reports, and strategic plans. If the parent corporation's product team is working on something
competitive, your information could (even unintentionally) flow to competitors within the parent
organization.

**4. Slow decision-making.** CVC investment decisions often require approval from the parent corp's
leadership, legal team, and corporate development group. This can make the process slower than
traditional VC.

**5. Blocking future deals.** Some CVCs require strategic rights — right of first refusal on
acquisition offers, co-development agreements, or exclusive licensing. These clauses can make future
fundraises complicated or acquisitions by other companies difficult.

**How to handle CVC money:**
- Accept it as a **follow-on investor** (not the lead) in a round led by a traditional VC.
- Negotiate to ensure the CVC has **no strategic rights** that could constrain your company.
- Ensure there's a **firewall** between the CVC's investment team and the parent corp's competitive
  product teams.
- Be comfortable with the signaling implications.

## 4.6 Government Grants and Non-Dilutive Funding

### What Non-Dilutive Funding Is

**Non-dilutive funding** is money you receive without giving up equity. It's "free money" in the sense
that you don't dilute your ownership. It comes from government programs, research grants, competitions,
and economic development incentives.

### SBIR and STTR Grants

The **Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR)** and **Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR)**
programs are the largest sources of non-dilutive funding for technology startups in the United States.
They are administered by 11 federal agencies, with the Department of Defense (DoD), National Science
Foundation (NSF), and National Institutes of Health (NIH) being the largest.

**How they work:**

Phase I grants are $50K–$275K (depending on the agency) for 6–12 months of feasibility research. The
purpose is to demonstrate that your technology concept is viable.

Phase II grants are $500K–$1.5M for 2 years of full development. You've proven the concept in Phase I;
now you build a working prototype or product.

Phase III is commercialization — no federal funding, but agencies may issue contracts for products
developed under SBIR/STTR.

**Why SBIR/STTR matters:**
- It's non-dilutive — you keep all your equity.
- The money is significant ($750K–$1.75M total across Phase I and II).
- The application process is competitive but straightforward (proposal-based, not investor-based).
- Having an SBIR/STTR grant signals technical credibility.
- Some VCs specifically look for companies with SBIR/STTR grants as a sign of technology validation.

**The catch:** SBIR/STTR grants come with strings — federal reporting requirements, compliance
obligations, and restrictions on what the money can be used for. The application process takes 3–6
months from submission to award, so it's not fast capital. And the topics are constrained: the
agency lists specific areas of interest, and your technology needs to align.

### Other Non-Dilutive Sources

**NSF I-Corps:** A $50K grant plus a 7-week entrepreneurship program. Designed to help academic
researchers validate the commercial potential of their technology. If you're a university spinout, this
is a great starting point.

**State and local economic development grants.** Many states and cities offer grants, tax credits, or
low-interest loans to startups. These vary wildly by location. Check with your state's economic
development agency.

**Competitions and prizes.** MIT 100K, TechCrunch Battlefield, XPRIZE challenges, and hundreds of
university and industry competitions award non-dilutive capital. The amounts are usually small ($10K–
$100K), but the exposure and validation can be valuable.

**Revenue-based financing.** Not technically a grant, but worth mentioning. Companies like Pipe,
Clearco, and Lighter Capital advance you capital based on your recurring revenue, which you repay as
a percentage of future revenue. No equity dilution, but it's still debt that must be repaid.

## 4.7 Syndicates and SPVs: Pooled Angel Money

### What Syndicates Are

An **angel syndicate** is a group of investors organized behind a **lead angel** who identifies deals,
performs diligence, and makes the investment decision. The other investors (called "backers") invest
alongside the lead angel, pooling their money into a single vehicle.

Syndicates are typically organized through platforms like **AngelList** (now AngelList Venture or
AngelList Syndicates). The mechanics:

1. The lead angel identifies a deal and decides to invest.
2. The lead shares the deal with their syndicate — a group of backers who have signed up to hear
   about their deals.
3. Backers choose whether to participate and specify how much to invest.
4. The total investment is pooled into a **Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV)** — a single-use legal entity
   created solely to hold this one investment.
5. The SPV appears as one line on your cap table, even though it may represent 20–100 individual
   investors.

### Why SPVs Matter

**For you (the founder):** An SPV keeps your cap table clean. Instead of 50 individual angels each
getting their own line, you have one SPV. This dramatically simplifies corporate governance — one
entity to send documents to, one entity to get signatures from.

**For the investors:** The SPV gives small investors access to deals they couldn't access individually.
A backer with $5K can participate in a deal that has a $100K minimum by pooling with others.

**For the lead:** The lead angel typically charges the SPV a **carry** (usually 15–20% of profits) and
sometimes a small management fee. This is how professional syndicate leads make money.

### Considerations

- Make sure the SPV has a single person you can contact for governance matters.
- Understand who the lead is and what their reputation is — their name is on your cap table.
- SPVs can be slow to fund (backers need time to commit), so build in timing buffer.

## 4.8 Crowdfunding: The Public Route

### Equity Crowdfunding

Under **Regulation Crowdfunding (Reg CF)** and **Regulation A+ (Reg A+),** startups can raise money
from the general public — not just accredited investors — through registered online platforms.

**Reg CF:**
- Maximum raise: $5M in a 12-month period
- Available to any U.S. company
- Investors can be anyone (accredited or non-accredited)
- Must use a registered intermediary (like Wefunder, Republic, or StartEngine)
- Requires audited financials above certain thresholds
- Creates potentially thousands of tiny shareholders

**Reg A+ (Mini-IPO):**
- Maximum raise: $75M in a 12-month period (Tier 2)
- Requires SEC qualification (not full registration, but close)
- Available to the public
- More expensive and complex than Reg CF

### Should You Consider Crowdfunding?

**Pros:**
- Non-traditional capital source (if VCs pass, the crowd might not)
- Marketing benefit (turning customers into investors creates evangelists)
- No single investor has power over your cap table

**Cons:**
- Massive cap table fragmentation (hundreds or thousands of shareholders)
- Regulatory complexity and cost
- Time-intensive campaign management
- Most VCs view crowdfunded companies skeptically — they may see it as a sign that institutional
  investors passed
- You must publicly disclose financial information (competitors can see it)

For most VC-track startups, equity crowdfunding is not the primary path. But for consumer brands and
companies with passionate communities, it can be a powerful complement to institutional capital.

## 4.9 How to Read an Investor's True Priorities

### The Hidden Hierarchy of Investor Preferences

Every investor — regardless of type — has a hierarchy of priorities that they may not explicitly
state. Understanding this hierarchy lets you tailor your pitch, negotiate more effectively, and
choose the right investors for your company.

**Tier 1 priorities (must-haves):**
- Market size (is this a venture-scale opportunity?)
- Team quality (can these people build this?)
- Timing (why now?)

**Tier 2 priorities (strong-wants):**
- Traction (is there evidence this works?)
- Competitive position (can you win?)
- Investment terms (is the price fair for the risk?)

**Tier 3 priorities (nice-to-haves that matter more than you'd think):**
- **Thesis fit.** Does this investment fit the story the GP tells their LPs about how the fund makes
  money? A healthcare-focused fund can't invest in your social media app, even if they love it.
- **Portfolio balance.** If the fund already invested in a competitor (or adjacent company), they
  likely can't invest in you. This isn't personal — it's a conflict of interest.
- **GP personal interests.** VCs are human. A GP who's personally passionate about climate tech will
  fight harder for a climate deal internally. A GP who doesn't understand your domain will be a
  weaker champion. Pick investors who genuinely care about your space.
- **Follow-on dynamics.** Can this investor lead the next round? Can they attract other investors who
  can? Your Series A investor should have relationships with Series B investors.
- **Brand building for the fund.** VCs care about their brand — both for LP fundraising and deal
  sourcing. Backing a "hot" company in a trendy space burnishes the fund's reputation. Being
  associated with a category-defining company is career-making for a GP.

### The Signaling Game

One of the most insidious dynamics in venture capital is **signaling.** If a well-known investor backs
your seed round and then doesn't participate in your Series A, other investors interpret this as a
negative signal: "Your own investor doesn't want to re-invest — they must know something bad."

This is irrational but real. Your seed investor might not re-invest for perfectly benign reasons (their
fund doesn't do Series A, they're out of capital, they have a portfolio conflict). But other investors
don't know the reason — they just see the absence.

**How to manage signaling risk:**
- Choose seed investors who can (and typically do) follow on.
- If a known investor passes on your next round, proactively explain why to prospective new investors.
  Don't let them assume the worst.
- Consider the "signaling discount" when choosing high-profile investors: the upside of their brand
  must outweigh the downside if they don't re-invest.

---

## 4.10 Investor Red Flags: When to Walk Away from Money

Not all money is created equal. Here are red flags that should make you pause or walk away:

**1. Excessive control demands.** An investor at the seed stage demanding two board seats, veto rights
over hiring, and approval of all expenditures above $10K is a control freak, not a partner.

**2. Unrealistic return expectations.** An angel who expects guaranteed 10x in three years doesn't
understand startup investing. They'll be a nightmare shareholder when things get hard.

**3. No relevant experience or network.** If an investor brings nothing but money and has no
connections in your space, they're just capital. Make sure the rest of the terms are at least favorable.

**4. Bad reputation among founders.** Before accepting money from ANY investor, talk to 3–5 founders
they've previously invested in. Ask specific questions: "What happened when the company struggled?
Were they supportive or destructive? Did they honor their commitments?"

**5. Slow-walking the process.** If an investor says they're interested but takes months to make a
decision, they're either not interested (and won't say so) or they're indecisive by nature (and
will be indecisive as a board member). Set deadlines and enforce them.

**6. "Strategic" strings.** A CVC that demands exclusive access to your technology, right of first
refusal on acquisition, or co-development commitments is buying an option on your company,
not just making an investment. Understand what you're giving up.

**7. Party round syndrome.** A round with no lead investor — just a collection of small checks from
angels and small funds — can leave you with no one who's incentivized to help. In a crisis, you
need a lead investor who'll write a bridge check, make introductions, and provide counsel. If
nobody "owns" the deal, nobody will step up.

## 4.11 Chapter Summary

| Investor Type | Check Size | Decision Speed | Pros | Cons |
|--------------|-----------|---------------|------|------|
| VC Fund | $500K–$50M+ | 4–12 weeks | Resources, brand, follow-on | Demanding, slow, power law pressure |
| Angel | $5K–$500K | Days–weeks | Fast, personal, flexible | Small checks, variable quality |
| Accelerator | $50K–$500K | Selective admission | Network, mentorship, brand | Equity cost, time commitment |
| Family Office | $250K–$10M | Weeks–months | Patient, large checks | Slow, variable expertise |
| CVC | $500K–$25M | 4–16 weeks | Strategic value, resources | Conflicts, signaling, strings |
| SBIR/STTR | $50K–$1.5M | 3–6 months | Non-dilutive, credibility | Slow, restricted use, compliance |
| Syndicate/SPV | $200K–$2M | 2–6 weeks | Clean cap table, diverse backers | Dependent on lead quality |
| Crowdfunding | $50K–$5M | Campaign-based | Community, marketing | Fragmented cap table, VC skepticism |

The type of money you take shapes the trajectory of your company. Choose investors who align with your
stage, your market, and your values. The best investor relationships feel like partnerships; the
worst feel like hostage situations. Due diligence goes both ways — vet your investors as rigorously
as they vet you.

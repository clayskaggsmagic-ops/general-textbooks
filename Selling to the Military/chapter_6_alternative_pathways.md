# Chapter 6: Alternative Pathways — OTAs, CSOs, SBIR/STTR, and Innovation Programs

---

Here is the most important sentence in this entire textbook: **You do not have to use traditional FAR-based contracting to sell to the Department of Defense.**

If Chapter 5 made you nervous about the regulatory labyrinth of the FAR and DFARS, this chapter is the antidote. Over the past decade—and accelerating dramatically since 2025—the DoD has built an entire parallel infrastructure of acquisition pathways specifically designed to engage companies that have never sold to the government before. These pathways are faster, more flexible, and deliberately stripped of many of the regulatory burdens that make traditional procurement so daunting for startups.

This isn't charity. The DoD has built these alternative pathways because it has a problem: the technology it needs increasingly comes from the commercial sector, and the companies that build that technology won't tolerate a two-year procurement cycle, won't submit 400-page proposals, and won't restructure their accounting systems to comply with Cost Accounting Standards. The alternative pathways exist because the Pentagon realized that if it couldn't change the companies it needed, it would have to change the way it buys.

The March 6, 2025 memorandum from Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth—"Directing Modern Software Acquisition to Maximize Lethality"—made this official policy. The memo mandates the Software Acquisition Pathway as the preferred method for all DoD software programs and directs that Commercial Solutions Openings (CSOs) and Other Transactions (OTs) be the **default** solicitation and award approaches for software capabilities. Not an option. Not an alternative. The default. Traditional FAR-based contracts are now the fallback, not the first choice.

This chapter maps every alternative door into the defense market. By the end, you'll know which one fits your company's stage, technology, and ambition.

## Other Transaction Authority: The Great Workaround

Other Transaction Authority (OTA) is the single most important alternative to traditional FAR-based contracting, and it's the mechanism that has done more than any other to bring commercial technology companies into the defense ecosystem.

### What an OT Actually Is

An Other Transaction is a legally binding agreement that is explicitly **not** a contract, grant, or cooperative agreement as defined by federal procurement law. This matters because it means OTs are not subject to the Federal Acquisition Regulation. No FAR clauses. No DFARS supplements. No Cost Accounting Standards. No Truth in Negotiations Act. No mandatory flow-downs. The regulatory overhead that makes traditional contracts so burdensome for commercial companies simply doesn't apply.

The legal authority for OTs traces to 10 U.S.C. § 4022 (formerly § 2371b), which authorizes the DoD to carry out prototype projects "directly relevant to enhancing the mission effectiveness of military personnel and the supporting platforms, systems, components, or materials proposed to be acquired or developed" by the DoD.

The statutory definition of "prototype project" is broad. It includes:

- Proof of concept
- Models and pilots
- Reverse engineering
- Novel applications of commercial technologies for defense purposes
- Agile development activities
- Creation, design, development, demonstration, or testing of technical or operational utility
- Even innovative business processes

That last item is worth noting. A "prototype" under OT authority doesn't have to be hardware. It can be a software platform, a data pipeline, a new way of doing logistics, or a novel analytical capability. For software companies, this definition is expansive enough to encompass almost anything you'd want to build.

### Prototype OTs vs. Production OTs

There are two types of OTs, and the distinction matters enormously:

**Prototype OTs** fund the development and demonstration of new capabilities. They're the entry point—the mechanism that gets you in the door. Prototype OTs are typically smaller (though they can range from hundreds of thousands to hundreds of millions), faster to award, and designed to prove that something works.

**Production OTs (Follow-On)** are the payoff. Under 10 U.S.C. § 4022(f), a successful prototype OT can transition directly to a follow-on production contract or transaction—**without additional competition**—if three conditions are met:

1. Competitive procedures were used to select participants for the prototype project.
2. The prototype project was successfully completed (key technical goals met, success metrics achieved, or particularly favorable results demonstrated).
3. The possibility of a follow-on production award was stated in both the solicitation and the prototype agreement.

The FY2025 NDAA (Section 817) clarified that follow-on production awards can include contracts to "produce, sustain, or otherwise implement the results" of a successful prototype—broadening the scope beyond pure manufacturing to encompass sustainment, integration, and deployment.

This prototype-to-production pipeline is the single most powerful mechanism available to startups. If you can win a competitive prototype OT and successfully demonstrate your technology, you can transition to a sole-source production contract worth far more than the original prototype, without re-competing against anyone.

### The Nontraditional Defense Contractor Designation

The term "nontraditional defense contractor" has a precise legal definition: an entity that has not performed any DoD contract or subcontract subject to full Cost Accounting Standards (CAS) coverage for at least one year prior to the solicitation. If your company has never done a traditional, CAS-covered DoD contract, you're nontraditional.

This designation matters because certain OT awards require the participation of at least one nontraditional defense contractor, one small business, or a cost-sharing arrangement with a participating entity. Your status as a nontraditional contractor makes you a valuable partner in teaming arrangements—traditional defense primes need you on their team to access OT authority.

Additional benefits of nontraditional status:
- Your products and services may be treated as commercial without a formal commercial determination by the government.
- Reduced regulatory requirements and greater contracting flexibility.
- You are not required to adhere to government accounting standards under OTs.
- Payment milestones, terms, and intellectual property rights are negotiable rather than dictated by regulation.

### 2025: The Year OTs Went Mainstream

Three developments in 2025 pushed OTs from "alternative" to "preferred":

**Executive Order 14265 (April 9, 2025): "Modernizing Defense Acquisitions and Spurring Innovation in the Defense Industrial Base."** This EO directs the DoD to establish a "general preference" for OTs to expedite acquisitions and promote innovation. It explicitly aims to attract nontraditional defense contractors and leverage commercial best practices.

**FY2025 NDAA Provisions:**
- Section 816 changed the approval authority for prototype projects costing $100M–$500M from the agency's senior procurement executive to the head of the contracting activity—making large OTs easier to approve.
- Section 817 expanded the scope of follow-on production to include sustainment and implementation.
- Section 888 requires the DoD to track OT awards to small businesses and nontraditional contractors—creating accountability for engaging the companies these pathways are designed to reach.

**The SecDef Software Memo (March 6, 2025):** As discussed above, this memo makes OTs the default for all DoD software acquisitions. DoD components are explicitly prohibited from implementing further restrictive measures beyond what's statutorily required.

## Commercial Solutions Openings: DIU's Front Door

The Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO) is the Defense Innovation Unit's primary mechanism for connecting commercial technology to military needs, and it is now—by directive of the Secretary of Defense—one of the two default approaches for DoD software acquisitions.

### How the CSO Process Works

Unlike traditional solicitations, where the government defines exactly what it wants and companies compete to deliver it, the CSO process starts with a problem statement and invites commercial companies to propose solutions. The process has four phases:

**Phase 0 — Area of Interest Publication.** DIU identifies critical national security challenges that can be addressed by commercial technology and publishes them as "Areas of Interest" (AOIs) on the DIU website and SAM.gov. These are open solicitations—they remain active for up to five years, with current listings running through early 2027—and companies can submit at any time. DIU's current priority areas include autonomy, AI/machine learning, cyber defense, advanced materials, commercial space systems, digital engineering, biotechnology, energy solutions, and sensing platforms.

**Phase 1 — Solution Brief.** Companies submit a short, commercially oriented technical pitch—typically a five-page white paper or 15-slide deck. No 400-page proposal. No compliance matrix. You describe your innovation, its scalability, its dual-use value, and its military application. DIU and its DoD partners review these for technical feasibility, relevance, and innovation.

**Phase 2 — Company Pitch.** If your solution brief passes review, you're invited to present a live pitch to DIU and DoD subject matter experts. This is a demonstration-focused session: show the product, explain the implementation timeline, present your pricing. Think venture capital pitch, not government proposal.

**Phase 3 — Prototype Agreement.** If the pitch succeeds, DIU requests a formal proposal and collaboratively develops an Other Transaction agreement for a prototype project with the company and the DoD partner.

### Speed and Scale

DIU aims to award prototype agreements in 60–90 days from initial engagement, though the actual average is closer to 120 days. Compare this to traditional procurement timelines of 12–24 months for a competitive contract—or 5–10 years for a major program of record.

A successful DIU prototype can transition to a non-competitive follow-on production contract with **any** DoD entity—not just the original sponsoring unit. This is critical: a prototype you developed with one Army brigade can become a production contract awarded by a completely different command.

### What DIU Doesn't Require

- No prior government contracting experience.
- No DoD facility clearances to begin engagement.
- No compliance with FAR or government accounting standards.
- Negotiable IP rights (you don't automatically surrender your intellectual property).

DIU is, by design, the friendliest door into the DoD for companies that have never worked with the government before.

## SBIR and STTR: The Government's Venture Fund

The Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) programs are the largest source of early-stage, non-dilutive funding available to technology startups in the United States. Across all participating federal agencies, the programs distribute roughly $4 billion per year. For defense-focused startups, the DoD's share—administered through each military service and defense agency—is by far the largest.

### The Three Phases

**Phase I — Feasibility.** Phase I awards fund the initial exploration of a technical concept's feasibility. As of 2025, agencies can issue Phase I awards up to approximately $314,000 without a separate SBA waiver, though agency-specific programs often set typical amounts lower (the DoD norm is $50,000–$250,000 depending on the soliciting component). Phase I typically runs 6–12 months.

**Phase II — Prototype Development.** Phase II takes a successful Phase I concept and develops it into a working prototype. Awards can reach approximately $2.1 million without a waiver, with timelines of 1–3 years. This is where you build the thing, test it, and demonstrate that it works.

**Phase III — Commercialization and Production.** Phase III is where the SBIR program's true power becomes apparent—and where most founders make a critical mistake by not understanding it.

Phase III is **not funded by SBIR program dollars.** Instead, it's funded by the agency's regular procurement budget, by other government funds, or by private capital. Here's why this matters:

- There is **no limitation** on the number, duration, type, or dollar value of a Phase III award.
- Phase III awards can be made as **sole-source contracts without additional competition**, because the competitive requirements were satisfied by the original Phase I and Phase II competitions.
- Federal agencies are mandated, to the greatest extent practicable, to issue Phase III awards to the SBIR awardee that developed the technology.
- Phase III awards can include production, services, R&D, testing, or any combination.
- There is **no time limit** between Phase II completion and Phase III award—a company can receive a Phase III contract years after its original SBIR work.
- Companies that have outgrown small business size standards can still receive Phase III awards.

Phase III is, in effect, a government-sanctioned pathway to sole-source production contracts of unlimited value. The competitive "ticket" you punched in Phase I and Phase II stays valid indefinitely. A $250,000 Phase I award can lead to a $50 million Phase III production contract—and there's nothing unusual about that trajectory.

### Where to Find SBIR/STTR Solicitations

- **SBIR.gov** — The central portal for all federal SBIR/STTR solicitations.
- **AFWERX.com** — The Air Force's innovation arm, which runs one of the most active SBIR programs in the DoD.
- **Army xTech** — The Army's open innovation program, which uses a competition format.
- **NavalX** — The Navy's innovation accelerator.
- **SpaceWERX** — The Space Force's SBIR portal.
- Individual defense agency websites (DARPA, MDA, DHA, etc.).

### The Open Topic Model

Traditional SBIR solicitations describe a specific technical problem and ask companies to propose solutions. The "open topic" model—pioneered by AFWERX in 2018—flips this. An open topic solicitation accepts innovative ideas related to the agency's broad mission areas without specifying a particular problem. You propose a dual-use technology that you've already developed (or are developing), and the evaluators decide if it has defense application.

The open topic model is enormously important for software companies because it lets you bring your existing commercial product to the DoD rather than building something from scratch to meet a government-defined specification. You're saying, "Here's what we've built. Here's why it matters for national security." The evaluators decide if they agree.

## STRATFI and TACFI: Bridging the Valley of Death

The "valley of death" is the gap between a successful SBIR Phase II prototype and a funded Phase III production contract. Many companies build impressive technology with SBIR money, then starve while waiting for a production opportunity. The Air Force created STRATFI and TACFI to bridge that gap.

### STRATFI (Strategic Funding Increase)

STRATFI targets large-scale, strategic capabilities at the Department of the Air Force level. Awards range from $3 million to $15 million in SBIR/STTR funds over a four-year period. But the key feature is the matching requirement: for every $1 of SBIR/STTR funds, companies must secure $2 from other government sources, or $1 from government sources plus $2 from private investment.

This matching structure is intentional. It ensures that STRATFI-funded companies have validation from both the military customer (who commits government funds) and private investors (who commit capital). A STRATFI award is, in effect, a triple endorsement: the SBIR program believes in your technology, an operational military unit believes in your technology, and private investors believe in your technology.

AFWERX selected 23 small businesses for the Program Year 2025.1 STRATFI class in March 2025. SpaceWERX launched its PY26.1 STRATFI with a rolling submission and evaluation system to increase flexibility.

### TACFI (Tactical Funding Increase)

TACFI is STRATFI's smaller sibling, focused on transitioning tactical capabilities. Awards range from $375,000 to $2 million in SBIR/STTR funds over a two-year period. The matching requirement is simpler: $1 of non-SBIR government or private funding for every $1 of SBIR funds.

TACFI moves faster and is designed for capabilities that are closer to operational readiness—things that a specific unit needs soon and that don't require the enterprise-level buy-in of a STRATFI.

### Eligibility

To be eligible for STRATFI or TACFI, your company must have an active SBIR/STTR Phase II contract or have completed one within the last two years. A government Point of Contact—typically the military end-user who wants your technology—submits the application package on your behalf. This means you need a champion inside the DoD who believes in your product enough to do the paperwork.

### Reauthorization Watch

The SBIR/STTR program authorization expired on October 1, 2025. Ongoing awards remain valid, but new solicitations are paused pending Congressional reauthorization. Congress is considering a proposal for a DoD-wide "Strategic Breakthrough Funding" program that would expand the STRATFI model and allow milestone-driven Phase II awards up to $30 million for critical technologies. This is worth watching closely: if passed, it would create the largest acceleration pathway yet for defense technology startups.

## Broad Agency Announcements: Funding for Research

Broad Agency Announcements (BAAs) are competitive solicitations for basic and applied research governed by FAR 35.016. They differ from RFPs in a critical way: BAAs don't specify what the government wants to buy. Instead, they describe broadly defined areas of research interest and invite proposals that advance scientific understanding.

### How BAAs Work

Agencies publish BAAs on SAM.gov, describing research areas of interest and evaluation criteria. Many BAAs are "open"—accepting proposals on a rolling basis rather than having a single deadline. Proposals are evaluated through peer or scientific review, with scientific merit as the primary factor. Proposals are not evaluated against each other but on their individual merit and relevance.

Awards resulting from BAAs can take various forms: contracts, grants, cooperative agreements, or OTs. Using a BAA fulfills the statutory requirement for full and open competition.

### Why BAAs Matter for Startups

BAAs are most relevant if your technology is at an early maturity level—Technology Readiness Level (TRL) 1–4—and you need funding to advance the science before you can build a prototype. They're particularly useful for companies with novel AI algorithms, new materials, advanced sensing approaches, or other technologies where the fundamental research isn't complete.

The most important BAAs for defense startups include:

- **DARPA BAAs** — DARPA publishes BAAs for each of its programs, and proposals can be submitted by companies of any size. DARPA's program manager model means that if a PM is interested in your approach, they have significant authority to fund it.
- **AFRL BAAs** — The Air Force Research Laboratory publishes both conventional and "Tactical" BAAs covering its full research portfolio.
- **ARL BAAs** — The Army Research Laboratory runs open BAAs across its core research areas.
- **ONR BAAs** — The Office of Naval Research publishes BAAs covering both basic and applied research.

## Challenge Competitions and Prize Programs

The DoD runs dozens of competition-based programs that provide startups with prize money, visibility, and—most importantly—connections to military end-users.

### xTech (Army)

The Army's xTechSearch program is a multi-phase open innovation competition that evaluates technologies from small businesses for Army applications. Companies pitch their technology through successive rounds, with prize money and increasing access at each stage. Winners can receive follow-on SBIR Phase II contracts and introductions to Army program managers.

The xTech program has expanded significantly, with specialized competitions including xTechHumanoid (autonomous robotics), xTech|Edge Strike (autonomous ground systems), and topic-specific challenges aligned with Army modernization priorities. Prize pools vary, but the real value is the direct connection to acquisition decision-makers.

### Hacking for Defense (H4D)

Hacking for Defense is a university-based program (modeled on Lean Startup methodology) where student teams work on real DoD problem statements provided by military sponsors. It doesn't directly fund startups, but it matters because it creates a pipeline of validated problems and potential solutions. If a H4D team identifies your technology as a solution to a DoD problem, that's a warm introduction you couldn't buy.

### Hack the Pentagon

Launched in 2016, Hack the Pentagon was the DoD's pioneering bug bounty program. It has expanded into a sustained vulnerability disclosure program where security researchers are paid to find flaws in DoD systems. If you're a cybersecurity company, participating in or supporting these programs builds credibility and relationships within the DoD cybersecurity community.

### The Replicator Program and Drone Dominance

The Replicator program, launched in August 2023, was the DoD's initiative to field thousands of low-cost, attritable autonomous systems across all domains within 18–24 months. Funded at approximately $1 billion across FY2024–FY2025, Replicator targeted uncrewed surface vehicles, aerial systems, and counter-drone capabilities. By late 2025, hundreds of systems had been fielded and thousands more put on contract.

In February 2026, the DoD escalated this effort with the Drone Dominance Program (DDP)—a $1.1 billion initiative to field low-cost, weaponized one-way attack drones through a competitive "Gauntlet" process testing capabilities and scaling production.

Both programs represent the DoD's willingness to move at commercial speed and at commercial scale for autonomous systems. If your technology fits these domains, these are programs where acquisition timelines are measured in months, not years.

## Consortia-Based OTAs: The Membership Model

Consortia-based OTAs add a layer of organization between the government and individual companies. A consortium is a membership organization—typically a nonprofit or accelerator—that manages an OT agreement with a government customer and then solicits, evaluates, and awards individual projects to its member companies.

### How They Work

1. The government establishes a "base" OT agreement with the consortium management organization.
2. The consortium recruits member companies—traditional and nontraditional, large and small, defense and commercial.
3. When the government has a need, it issues a "Request for Solutions" (RFS) or "Request for White Papers" through the consortium.
4. Member companies submit short proposals. The consortium evaluates them (often with government input) and awards individual project agreements under the base OT.
5. The consortium handles administrative overhead: contracting, payments, compliance.

This model is attractive for startups because the consortium absorbs much of the administrative burden and provides a streamlined pathway to OT awards. Membership typically requires demonstrating a technical contribution to the consortium's focus area.

### Key Consortia

**NSTXL (National Security Technology Accelerator)** manages several major OT vehicles:
- **S²MARTS (Strategic & Spectrum Missions Advanced Resilient Trusted Systems):** Created by Naval Surface Warfare Center Crane Division, focused on trusted microelectronics, electromagnetic spectrum, and strategic missions.
- **Space Enterprise Consortium (SpEC):** Rapid space prototyping for the Space Force.

**NSTIC (Naval Surface Technology & Innovation Consortium)** focuses on naval surface technology, covering advanced computing, software engineering, autonomous systems, directed energy, AI/ML, and cyber. It provides federally funded R&D opportunities specifically emphasizing small and emerging companies without prior government experience.

**Other notable consortia include:**
- **Advanced Technology International (ATI):** Manages several major OTs including for SOCOM and the Chemical/Biological Defense Program.
- **Consortium for Command, Control, and Communications in Cyberspace (C5):** Focused on Army cyber and IT.
- **National Armaments Consortium (NAC):** Focused on munitions and weapons systems.

### The Trade-Off

Consortia charge membership fees and take a management fee on awarded projects (typically 3–7% of the project value). This is the price of reduced administrative burden. For startups without a contracts department, this is usually a worthwhile trade.

## Deploying Through DoD Software Factories

If you're a software company, the DoD's software factory ecosystem is one of the most important concepts in this chapter—and one of the least understood outside the defense community.

### What Software Factories Are

DoD software factories are government-operated (often contractor-supported) DevSecOps platforms that provide standardized development, security, and deployment infrastructure for software applications. They exist because the DoD discovered that the traditional approach—where every program builds its own development pipeline, conducts its own security scanning, and navigates its own Authority to Operate (ATO) process—was impossibly slow and redundant.

Software factories centralize the hard parts: the CI/CD pipeline, the container security scanning, the vulnerability management, the compliance documentation, and the ATO process itself. The result is that software deployed through a software factory can reach operational use dramatically faster than software deployed through traditional channels.

### The Big Three

**Platform One (Air Force / Enterprise)**

Platform One is the DoD's enterprise-level DevSecOps platform and the most widely used software factory across the department. Its key components include:

- **Iron Bank:** The DoD's official repository of pre-approved, hardened container images. Iron Bank contains over 550 container images that have been scanned, vetted, and approved for use across the DoD. If your application uses a container image from Iron Bank, it inherits that image's security approval—dramatically reducing the work required for your own ATO.
- **Party Bus:** A managed DevSecOps environment providing CI/CD pipelines. Applications deployed through Party Bus can receive operational approval in as few as 30 days.
- **Big Bang:** An Infrastructure-as-Code solution that deploys full DevSecOps environments across classification levels. Big Bang can reduce the ATO timeline to approximately 90 days—compared to the traditional 18–24 months.
- **Repo One:** The DoD's centralized source code repository.

Platform One's continuous Authority to Operate (cATO) framework is the key innovation. Instead of a one-time security assessment that freezes your software at a point in time, cATO continuously monitors your application's security posture and allows ongoing updates. You can push new code, deploy new features, and iterate at commercial speed—while maintaining authorization.

**Black Pearl (Navy)**

Black Pearl is the Department of the Navy's secure DevSecOps platform. It partners with Platform One (using Iron Bank for containers and Repo One for source code) but operates with Navy-specific accreditation. Black Pearl is government-owned and contractor-operated, focused on delivering mission-ready software with Navy ATO.

**Other Software Factories:**

Nearly every service and major command now has its own software factory or uses Platform One. Army Software Factory, Kessel Run (Air Force), Overmatch Software Armory (Navy), and others provide similar capabilities within their respective domains.

### Building ON vs. Being Consumed BY

There's a critical distinction for startups:

**Building ON a software factory** means deploying your application using the factory's infrastructure. You containerize your application, get your container images approved in Iron Bank, deploy through Party Bus or Big Bang, and benefit from the factory's cATO. You maintain your own application; the factory provides the secure runway.

**Being consumed BY a software factory** means your software is integrated as a component of the factory's toolchain—becoming a tool that other developers across the DoD use. This is a different (and potentially larger) business opportunity: you're not deploying an end-user application, you're becoming infrastructure.

### Why Software Factories Matter for Your ATO Timeline

The traditional ATO process can take 18–24 months and cost millions of dollars in compliance documentation and security testing. For a startup, that timeline is a company-killer. Software factories compress this:

| Approach | Typical ATO Timeline |
|---|---|
| Traditional (standalone) | 18–24 months |
| Big Bang deployment | ~90 days |
| Party Bus deployment | ~30 days |
| cATO (continuous) | Ongoing after initial approval |

The DoD's FY2025–2026 Software Modernization Implementation Plan aims to make the software factory ecosystem the default for all DoD missions. A "Quick Track SaaS ATO Process" is being developed and piloted to further accelerate approvals. The July 2025 Department of the Navy containerization policy memorandum underscored the strategic importance of container-based deployment for security and development speed.

If you're building software for the DoD, the question isn't whether to engage with software factories—it's which one to engage with and how quickly you can get your containers into Iron Bank.

## The Decision Matrix: Which Door Is Right for You?

With this many pathways, the most common mistake is analysis paralysis—spending so much time figuring out which program to pursue that you don't pursue any of them. Here's a simplified framework:

### By Technology Maturity

| Your Stage | Best Pathway |
|---|---|
| **Pre-product (idea/research phase)** | BAA, SBIR Phase I |
| **Early prototype (TRL 3–5)** | SBIR Phase I → Phase II, DARPA BAA |
| **Working prototype (TRL 5–7)** | DIU CSO, SBIR Phase II → STRATFI/TACFI |
| **Production-ready product (TRL 7–9)** | DIU CSO → Follow-on production OT, SBIR Phase III |
| **Mature commercial product** | DIU CSO, GSA Schedule, direct OT via consortium |

### By Company Profile

| Your Situation | Best Pathway |
|---|---|
| **Never sold to government** | DIU CSO, AFWERX Open Topic SBIR, consortium OTA |
| **Active SBIR Phase II** | STRATFI/TACFI, Phase III sole-source |
| **Cybersecurity company** | Hack the Pentagon, DIU CSO (cyber AOIs), SBIR |
| **AI/ML company** | DIU CSO, DARPA BAA, Army xTech, SBIR |
| **Autonomous systems** | Replicator program, Drone Dominance, DIU CSO |
| **SaaS/cloud platform** | Platform One/Iron Bank, DIU CSO, Software Acquisition Pathway |

### By Timeline

| How Quickly You Need Revenue | Best Pathway |
|---|---|
| **< 6 months** | DIU CSO (if you match an active AOI), consortium OTA |
| **6–12 months** | SBIR Phase I, DIU CSO |
| **12–24 months** | SBIR Phase II, STRATFI/TACFI |
| **2+ years (building toward major contract)** | BAA → Prototype OT → Production OT |

### The Stacking Strategy

The most sophisticated defense startups don't pick one pathway—they stack them:

1. **Enter through SBIR Phase I** ($50K–$250K) to prove feasibility and build your first government relationship.
2. **Win SBIR Phase II** (~$1M–$2M) to develop a working prototype with a military end-user.
3. **Apply for STRATFI** ($3M–$15M + matching) to scale the prototype and attract private investment.
4. **Deploy through a software factory** (Platform One/Iron Bank) to accelerate your ATO.
5. **Pursue Phase III** sole-source production contract with the operational unit that loves your product.
6. **Simultaneously respond to DIU CSOs** in your technology area to open additional customer relationships across the DoD.

This stacking approach lets you build revenue incrementally, develop multiple government champions, and create a portfolio of contract vehicles that reduce your dependence on any single program.

## Common Mistakes in Alternative Pathways

### Treating SBIR as a Grant

SBIR awards are contracts, not grants. They come with deliverables, milestones, and reporting requirements. Companies that treat SBIR money as free R&D funding and don't deliver results will not get Phase II awards and will not be invited back.

### Ignoring Phase III

The biggest SBIR mistake is winning Phase I and Phase II and then failing to pursue Phase III. Phase III doesn't happen automatically—you have to identify a military customer, demonstrate that your technology meets their need, and work with their contracting office to execute a Phase III contract. The SBIR program gives you the right to sole-source; it doesn't give you the customer.

### Assuming the OT Protects You Forever

While OTs are exempt from FAR, follow-on production contracts may transition back to traditional FAR-based procurement, especially at scale. Companies that build no compliance infrastructure during the prototype phase sometimes get caught flat-footed when the production contract includes FAR clauses they've never seen before. Use the flexibility of the OT phase to build revenue and relationships, but keep building your compliance capability in parallel.

### Not Building the Government Relationship

Every alternative pathway requires a government champion—a program manager, end-user, or innovation officer who believes in your technology and is willing to fight internal bureaucracy to fund it. The technology alone is never enough. Chapter 9 will cover relationship-building in detail, but the principle applies here: before you submit to any of these programs, you should already have a conversation with someone in uniform (or in a government office) who wants what you're building.

## Key Terms

**OTA (Other Transaction Authority):** Legal authority under 10 U.S.C. § 4022 for DoD agreements not subject to FAR. Used for prototype and production projects.

**Prototype OT:** An Other Transaction agreement for developing and demonstrating new capabilities.

**Production OT (Follow-On):** A sole-source follow-on to a successful prototype OT for production, sustainment, or implementation.

**Nontraditional defense contractor:** An entity that has not performed a DoD contract or subcontract subject to full CAS coverage for at least one year.

**CSO (Commercial Solutions Opening):** DIU's solicitation mechanism for acquiring commercial technology through OTs. Now a default approach for DoD software.

**Software Acquisition Pathway (SWP):** DoD acquisition pathway mandated as the preferred method for all software programs by the March 2025 SecDef memo.

**SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research):** Federal program providing phased R&D funding to small businesses. Phase I (feasibility), Phase II (prototype), Phase III (production/commercialization).

**STTR (Small Business Technology Transfer):** Similar to SBIR but requires a research institution partnership.

**Phase III (SBIR):** Sole-source commercialization contracts with no competition, dollar, or time limitations. Funded by non-SBIR government funds or private capital.

**STRATFI (Strategic Funding Increase):** Air Force program bridging SBIR Phase II to Phase III. $3M–$15M over 4 years with matching requirements.

**TACFI (Tactical Funding Increase):** Smaller Air Force bridge program. $375K–$2M over 2 years with 1:1 matching.

**BAA (Broad Agency Announcement):** Competitive solicitation for basic and applied research under FAR 35.016.

**Consortium OTA:** An OT managed by a membership organization that administers projects to member companies on behalf of a government customer.

**NSTXL (National Security Technology Accelerator):** Consortium manager for S²MARTS and Space Enterprise Consortium (SpEC) OTs.

**NSTIC (Naval Surface Technology & Innovation Consortium):** Consortium focused on naval surface technology and innovation.

**Platform One:** DoD enterprise DevSecOps platform providing Iron Bank, Party Bus, Big Bang, and Repo One.

**Iron Bank:** DoD repository of 550+ pre-approved, hardened container images for secure software deployment.

**Party Bus:** Platform One's managed CI/CD environment enabling ~30-day operational approval.

**Big Bang:** Infrastructure-as-Code solution for deploying full DevSecOps environments, reducing ATO to ~90 days.

**Black Pearl:** Department of the Navy's secure DevSecOps platform, partnered with Platform One.

**cATO (Continuous Authority to Operate):** Ongoing security authorization framework allowing continuous software updates.

**Replicator Program:** DoD initiative ($1B FY24–25) to field thousands of autonomous systems at commercial speed.

**Drone Dominance Program:** $1.1B program (Feb 2026) for competitive development and production of low-cost attack drones.

**xTech:** Army open innovation competition connecting small businesses with military technology needs.

**Hacking for Defense (H4D):** University-based program using Lean Startup methodology to solve DoD problems.

**Valley of Death:** Gap between successful R&D and funded production—the stage where many defense startups fail.

**TRL (Technology Readiness Level):** Scale from 1 (basic principles observed) to 9 (operational system proven) measuring technology maturity.

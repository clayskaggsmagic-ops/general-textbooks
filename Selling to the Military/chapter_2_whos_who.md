# Chapter 2: Who's Who — The Organizations That Matter

---

## Why This Chapter Exists

Chapter 1 taught you how the machine works. This chapter teaches you who runs it.

The Department of Defense is not a single organization. It's an ecosystem of hundreds of organizations, offices, commands, and agencies—each with its own budget, its own authorities, its own culture, and its own priorities. Navigating this ecosystem is not optional. If you don't know who has the money, who has the authority, and who actually makes the buying decisions, you will waste years pitching the wrong people.

This is the chapter you should come back to repeatedly. Think of it as a reference map of the defense ecosystem. The first time you read it, you'll absorb the broad structure. The tenth time you reference it, you'll be looking up a specific office because you just got a meeting with someone who works there and you need to understand their organization before you walk through the door.

Let's start at the top and work our way through every organization that matters.

## The Military Services: Five Separate Buying Machines

The most important thing to understand about the military services is that each one is, for practical purposes, a separate acquisition enterprise. The Army buys things differently than the Navy. The Air Force has different priorities than the Marine Corps. The Space Force is building its acquisition infrastructure from scratch.

### The Department of the Army

The Army is the largest military service by personnel and one of the largest by budget ($197.4 billion in FY2026). Its acquisition system is managed by the **Assistant Secretary of the Army for Acquisition, Logistics, and Technology (ASA(ALT))**, who serves as the Army Acquisition Executive (AAE).

The Army's priorities have been shifting dramatically. For two decades, it was focused on counterinsurgency and ground operations in Iraq and Afghanistan. Now it's pivoting to **Large-Scale Combat Operations (LSCO)** against peer adversaries—meaning China and Russia. This has driven major investments in long-range precision fires, next-generation combat vehicles, network modernization, and soldier lethality.

**Key Army acquisition organizations:**

- **Army Futures Command (AFC):** Created in 2018, AFC is responsible for modernization—defining future requirements and driving the development of next-generation capabilities. AFC's eight Cross-Functional Teams (CFTs) cut across traditional organizational boundaries to accelerate capability delivery in priority areas like Long-Range Precision Fires, Next-Generation Combat Vehicles, and Network modernization. If your technology aligns with an AFC modernization priority, AFC is where the energy (and increasingly the money) is.

- **Army Materiel Command (AMC):** AMC handles the logistics, sustainment, and industrial base side. If your technology is fielded and needs to be maintained, repaired, or produced at scale, you'll interact with AMC.

- **Army Applications Lab (AAL):** The Army's innovation accelerator. AAL uses OTAs (Other Transaction Authorities) and simplified contracting to connect commercial technology companies with Army requirements. It's specifically designed for startups and nontraditional defense companies. AAL manages the Army's Venture Capital Initiative and runs pitch events where companies can present directly to Army decision-makers. If you're a startup trying to get your foot in the Army's door, AAL is one of your best entry points.

- **Army Software Factory:** A government-run software development organization that trains soldiers as software engineers and builds mission applications using modern DevSecOps practices. For commercial software companies, the Army Software Factory can be both a partner (if your tools integrate with their platform) and a competitor (if they decide to build internally). Understanding what the Army Software Factory is building—and where it needs commercial help—is important intelligence for any software startup targeting the Army.

As of November 2025, the Army has consolidated its PEOs into **six new Portfolio Acquisition Executive (PAE)** offices: Fires; Maneuver Ground; Maneuver Air; Command and Control & Counter-C2; Agile Sustainment & Ammunition; and Layered Protection & CBRN Defense. Each PAE oversees all aspects of its capability area from requirements through international sales—a much broader scope than the old PEO model.

### The Department of the Navy (including the Marine Corps)

The Navy's FY2026 budget of $292.2 billion encompasses both naval and Marine Corps operations. Navy acquisition is overseen by the **Assistant Secretary of the Navy for Research, Development, and Acquisition (ASN(RDA))**.

The Navy is consumed by a shipbuilding crisis. Its fleet has shrunk while its obligations have grown. Building, maintaining, and modernizing a fleet of aircraft carriers, submarines, surface combatants, and amphibious ships is extraordinarily expensive and takes decades. This creates both problems and opportunities for technology companies—the Navy desperately needs technologies that can extend the life of existing platforms, reduce maintenance costs, and deliver capability without building new hulls.

**Key Navy acquisition organizations:**

- **Naval Sea Systems Command (NAVSEA):** The largest of the Navy's systems commands, responsible for shipbuilding, ship maintenance, and most naval weapons programs. NAVSEA manages roughly $40 billion annually. If your technology touches ships, submarines, or naval weapons, NAVSEA is the elephant in the room.

- **Naval Air Systems Command (NAVAIR):** Responsible for the Navy and Marine Corps' aircraft, weapons, and avionics. NAVAIR manages programs like the F/A-18, F-35C, MQ-25, and the Navy's extensive rotary-wing fleet.

- **Naval Information Warfare Systems Command (NAVWAR):** The Navy's command for C4I (Command, Control, Communications, Computers, and Intelligence) systems, cybersecurity, and information warfare. Software companies are most likely to engage with NAVWAR, as it manages the Navy's IT infrastructure, tactical networks, and cyber capabilities.

- **Marine Corps Systems Command (MCSC):** The Marine Corps' acquisition organization, responsible for ground combat vehicles, infantry equipment, and C4I systems for Marines.

- **NavalX:** The Navy's innovation arm, equivalent to the Army's AAL. NavalX facilitates connections between commercial innovators and Navy requirements through tech bridges, prize challenges, and partnerships with university and industry partners.

- **Black Pearl:** The Navy's DevSecOps platform and software factory. Black Pearl provides the infrastructure for continuous software delivery in the naval domain, similar to what Platform One does for the Air Force. Commercial software companies that want to deploy to Navy environments will likely need to work within or alongside Black Pearl's ecosystem.

As of late 2025, the Navy has established five PAE organizations: Industrial Operations, Marine Corps, Maritime, Strategic Systems Programs, and Undersea—aligned with the Secretary of the Navy's "Golden Fleet" initiative.

### The Department of the Air Force (including the Space Force)

The Air Force's FY2026 budget of $301.1 billion makes it the single largest military budget. Air Force acquisition is managed by the **Assistant Secretary of the Air Force for Acquisition, Technology, and Logistics (SAF/AQ)**.

The Air Force has one of the most active innovation ecosystems in the DoD. It has embraced commercial technology adoption more aggressively than the other services, and its acquisition workforce includes some of the most reform-minded leaders in the Department.

**Key Air Force acquisition organizations:**

- **Air Force Materiel Command (AFMC):** The Air Force's equivalent of AMC—responsible for research, development, test and evaluation, and sustainment of air and space systems.

- **Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL):** One of the most important R&D organizations in the entire DoD. AFRL manages a broad research portfolio spanning materials, sensors, munitions, propulsion, and information systems. AFRL's research directorates are often the first point of contact for companies with breakthrough technologies.

- **AFWERX:** The Air Force's innovation hub. AFWERX is arguably the most active and accessible service-level innovation organization in the DoD. It manages three main programs:
  - **AFVentures:** The Air Force's SBIR/STTR portal, which has become one of the largest sources of innovation funding in the federal government. Under Open Topics, companies can pitch ideas that don't match a specific government solicitation but address Air Force needs.
  - **Spark:** An intrapreneurship program that empowers Airmen and Guardians to identify problems and connect with industry solutions.
  - **Prime:** Focused on scaling successful technologies from prototype to production.
  
  AFWERX has awarded thousands of SBIR contracts and is one of the most startup-friendly entry points in the entire defense ecosystem.

- **Platform One:** The Air Force's enterprise DevSecOps platform and one of the most important organizations in the DoD for software companies. Platform One provides the tools, infrastructure, and services that enable continuous software delivery across the Air Force and increasingly across the broader DoD. Its key components include:
  - **Iron Bank:** A DoD-hardened container repository. If you deliver containerized software, getting your containers into Iron Bank is a critical step toward deployment on DoD networks. Iron Bank containers are pre-scanned, pre-approved, and available for use across the Department—dramatically reducing the security approval burden for individual programs. Commercial companies can submit containers to Iron Bank for evaluation and inclusion in the repository.
  - **Party Bus:** Platform One's managed DevSecOps pipeline-as-a-service, providing CI/CD infrastructure that meets DoD security requirements.
  - **Big Bang:** A deployment environment built on Kubernetes that integrates the security and compliance tooling needed for DoD environments.
  
  For a software startup, understanding Platform One is essential. Getting your product into Iron Bank, or building your application to deploy on Platform One's infrastructure, can dramatically accelerate your path to production in the Air Force and beyond.

- **Kessel Run:** The Air Force's pioneering software factory, originally created to modernize air operations center software. Kessel Run demonstrated that the military could build and deliver software using Agile and DevSecOps practices at a pace comparable to commercial software companies. While Kessel Run focuses primarily on internal development, its model has influenced every subsequent DoD software factory, and it partners with commercial companies for tooling, infrastructure, and specialized capabilities.

As of December 2025, the Air Force designated five initial PEOs as PAEs, with additional offices transitioning in phases. The Space Force is restructuring to replace PEOs with seven PAE portfolios.

### The United States Space Force

The Space Force ($40 billion in FY2026, up 30%+ from FY2025) is the newest and fastest-growing military service. Created in December 2019, it's still building its organizational infrastructure—which means the acquisition landscape is more fluid and potentially more accessible than the other services.

**Key Space Force organizations:**

- **Space Systems Command (SSC):** The Space Force's primary acquisition organization, responsible for developing and acquiring space systems including satellites, launch services, ground systems, and space domain awareness capabilities.

- **SpaceWERX:** The Space Force's innovation arm, modeled after AFWERX. SpaceWERX runs SBIR/STTR programs, Pitch Days, and Tactical Funding Increases (TACFIs) to connect commercial space and technology companies with Space Force needs. The commercial space industry is booming, and SpaceWERX is actively seeking companies that can contribute to space domain awareness, satellite communications, cybersecurity for space systems, and commercial space integration.

The Space Force's rapid budget growth and organizational newness make it one of the most interesting opportunities for technology startups. The culture is less entrenched than the older services, and the leadership has explicitly stated its desire to work with commercial and nontraditional companies.

## The Office of the Secretary of Defense: Where Policy Becomes Power

The OSD is the Defense Secretary's staff organization. It doesn't build or buy things directly—but it sets the policies, priorities, and rules that determine how everything gets built and bought. Several OSD offices are critically important for technology companies.

### The Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition and Sustainment (USD(A&S))

The USD(A&S) is the **Defense Acquisition Executive (DAE)**—the most senior acquisition official in the Department of Defense. This office:

- Sets acquisition policy (including the Adaptive Acquisition Framework)
- Serves as the Milestone Decision Authority for the largest programs (ACAT ID)
- Chairs the Defense Acquisition Board
- Oversees the defense industrial base
- Manages the disposition of surplus military property

For founders, the USD(A&S) matters because this office shapes the rules of the game. Policy decisions made here—like the March 2025 SecDef memorandum mandating the Software Acquisition Pathway—flow down through the entire system and determine what contracting mechanisms are available, what oversight is required, and what pathways program offices can use.

The November 2025 "Transforming the Warfighting Acquisition System" directive from the Secretary of War originated from USD(A&S) and mandated the transition to Portfolio Acquisition Executives across all services within two years. This flattened the acquisition chain of authority: **DAE → Service Acquisition Executives → PAEs**, removing intermediate layers to accelerate decision-making.

### The Under Secretary of Defense for Research and Engineering (USD(R&E))

The USD(R&E) is the DoD's chief technology officer, responsible for:

- Setting the direction for defense research and engineering
- Overseeing DARPA
- Managing the DoD's science and technology (S&T) portfolio
- Identifying and prioritizing critical technology areas
- As of August 2025, overseeing the Chief Digital and AI Officer (CDAO)

The USD(R&E) publishes the **Critical Technology Areas** list—the technologies the DoD considers most important for national security. If your technology falls on this list, it's a strong signal that there's institutional energy (and money) behind it. As of 2025, the list emphasizes areas including advanced computing, AI/ML, autonomy, biotechnology, directed energy, FutureG communications, hypersonics, microelectronics, quantum science, space technology, and trusted AI.

### The Chief Digital and AI Officer (CDAO)

The CDAO deserves special attention because it is the single most important organization in the DoD for software and AI companies.

Created in late 2021 by consolidating several previously separate organizations (including the Joint AI Center and the Defense Digital Service), the CDAO was established to serve as the DoD's primary lead for data, analytics, artificial intelligence, and software modernization.

**What the CDAO does:**

- Sets AI and data policy for the entire DoD
- Manages the DoD's AI/ML strategy, including responsible AI implementation
- Oversees the adoption of modern software practices across the Department
- Runs the AI testing and evaluation infrastructure
- Coordinates AI/ML operations and model management across services
- Manages the DoD's data strategy and Chief Data Officer functions

**Budget and organizational details:**

The CDAO's FY2025 budget allocation was **$139.9 million** specifically for accelerating AI and machine learning initiatives. This funded priorities including AI testing and evaluation, AI/ML operations (MLOps), model management infrastructure, and the ethical implementation of AI.

As of **August 14, 2025**, the CDAO was realigned to report directly to the USD(R&E). Deputy Secretary of Defense Stephen Feinberg directed this change as part of the Pentagon's "AI-first" strategy, stating it would create a "powerful innovation engine" capable of delivering AI superiority from lab to battlefield. This is significant: by placing the CDAO under the same umbrella as DARPA and the broader S&T enterprise, the DoD is integrating AI/software modernization directly into its technology pipeline rather than keeping it as a standalone initiative.

**Why software startups should care:**

The CDAO's priorities become the DoD's priorities for AI and software. Understanding what the CDAO is investing in—generative AI applications, autonomous systems, data mesh architectures, AI assurance frameworks—tells you where the institutional energy is flowing. The CDAO also influences how other organizations across the DoD adopt software and AI, meaning its policy decisions affect your ability to sell to Army, Navy, Air Force, and Space Force customers.

If you're building AI/ML tools, data platforms, or software infrastructure, the CDAO should be on your radar as both a potential customer and as the policy-setting body whose priorities shape demand across the entire Department.

### The DoD Chief Information Officer (DoD CIO)

The DoD CIO is responsible for the Department's information technology infrastructure, cybersecurity policy, and digital modernization strategy. While the CDAO focuses on AI, data, and software development practices, the CIO focuses on the underlying IT infrastructure, networks, and cybersecurity compliance requirements.

**What the CIO controls that matters to you:**

- Cybersecurity policy and the CMMC (Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification) framework
- Cloud computing policy (in coordination with DISA)
- The DoD Information Network (DoDIN) and its security requirements
- Zero Trust Architecture implementation across the DoD
- IT standards that affect how your software must be built and deployed

If the CDAO tells the DoD *what* software to build, the CIO tells the DoD *how* to secure and deploy it. Both matter for software companies, but the CIO's cybersecurity requirements are more likely to create compliance obligations that you must meet before your software can operate on DoD networks.

## Program Executive Offices and the PAE Transition

### What PEOs Were (and Why They're Changing)

Program Executive Offices were—until recently—the workhorses of defense acquisition. A PEO was a mid-level organization responsible for managing a portfolio of related acquisition programs. Each PEO had a Program Executive Officer—a senior civilian or military official—who oversaw multiple Program Managers (PMs).

For example, **PEO Soldier** managed all the Army programs related to individual soldier equipment—body armor, night vision, helmets, communications gear. **PEO Missiles and Space** managed missile defense and space-related Army programs. **PEO Aviation** managed Army helicopters and drones.

PEOs were important for founders because the PEO is where the actual buying authority sits. Program Managers within PEOs control budgets, issue contracts, and make decisions about which technologies to adopt. Knowing which PEO owns the problem you're solving—and building a relationship with the right PM within that PEO—is one of the most important things a defense tech company can do.

### The Transition to Portfolio Acquisition Executives (PAEs)

Starting in late 2025, the DoD began a fundamental restructuring of its acquisition management, transitioning from the PEO model to **Portfolio Acquisition Executives (PAEs)**. This was mandated by Section 201 of the FoRGED Act and Section 1802 of the FY2026 NDAA, and formalized through the Secretary of War's November 2025 "Transforming the Warfighting Acquisition System" directive.

**What's changing:**

| PEO Model | PAE Model |
|---|---|
| Managed specific programs or narrow sets of similar programs | Manages a broad portfolio of capabilities |
| Authority limited to acquisition execution | Authority encompasses requirements, programming, AND acquisition |
| Intermediate layers between program and senior leadership | Flattened hierarchy: DAE → SAE → PAE |
| Program-centric optimization | Portfolio-level optimization across entire capability areas |

The key innovation of the PAE model is **unified authority**. A PAE doesn't just manage the acquisition of systems—they control the requirements process and the programming (budget allocation) process for their entire capability area. This means a single person can make trade-offs across an entire portfolio: reallocate funds from a low-performing program to a high-performing one, adjust requirements based on emerging threats, and prioritize speed-to-field over bureaucratic compliance.

**What this means for founders:**

Practically, this is good news. The PAE model concentrates decision-making authority and reduces the number of organizational layers between your technology and the person who can decide to buy it. A PAE who controls requirements, budget, and acquisition for an entire capability area has more flexibility to make pragmatic decisions—including decisions to adopt commercial technology from nontraditional suppliers.

However, the transition is ongoing. As of early 2026, each service is at a different stage of implementation:
- **Army:** Six PAE offices operational
- **Air Force:** Five initial PAEs designated, with more transitioning
- **Navy:** Five PAE organizations established
- **Space Force:** Seven of nine planned PAE portfolios being structured

During the transition period, you may encounter organizations that are still using the PEO structure while others have moved to PAEs. Your job remains the same either way: find the person who owns the problem you solve, understand their budget and authority, and build a relationship.

## DARPA: The Engine of Breakthrough Technology

The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency is one of the most storied organizations in the federal government. It created the internet. It funded the research that led to GPS, stealth aircraft, mRNA vaccines, and modern AI. DARPA's mission is to prevent and create technological surprise—to ensure the U.S. military is never blindsided by a new technology and, when possible, to be the one doing the blindsiding.

### How DARPA Works

DARPA is small by DoD standards—roughly 220 employees—but punches enormously above its weight. Its organizational model is built around **Program Managers (PMs)**, who are the soul of the agency.

**The Program Manager Model**

DARPA PMs are typically hired from academia, industry, or the military on limited-term appointments (usually 3-5 years). They are not career bureaucrats. They are domain experts with a vision for a specific technological breakthrough. When they arrive at DARPA, they develop programs—collections of funded research projects designed to achieve a specific technical goal—and manage those programs for their tenure.

The PM has extraordinary autonomy. Unlike program managers in the traditional acquisition system, DARPA PMs can define their own research programs, select performers, and manage contracts with minimal bureaucratic oversight. The agency's culture explicitly encourages risk-taking and tolerates failure. A DARPA PM who plays it safe and funds only incremental research is considered to have failed. A DARPA PM who funds an audacious program that doesn't work but pushes the boundaries of knowledge is considered to have done their job.

**DARPA's six technical offices manage different domains:**

| Office | Focus Areas |
|---|---|
| **Biological Technologies Office (BTO)** | Biotech, synthetic biology, neurotechnology |
| **Defense Sciences Office (DSO)** | Foundational science, mathematics, materials |
| **Information Innovation Office (I2O)** | AI, cyber, software, data analytics, communications |
| **Microsystems Technology Office (MTO)** | Microelectronics, photonics, MEMS |
| **Strategic Technology Office (STO)** | Electronic warfare, ISR, strategic systems |
| **Tactical Technology Office (TTO)** | Platforms, weapons, autonomy |

For software and AI companies, the **Information Innovation Office (I2O)** is the most relevant. I2O programs cover AI, human-machine teaming, cybersecurity, distributed systems, and information operations.

### How DARPA Funds Things

DARPA primarily uses two mechanisms:

1. **Broad Agency Announcements (BAAs):** These are the standard DARPA solicitation. A PM publishes a BAA describing the technical problem they want to solve, and companies, universities, and research labs submit proposals. BAAs can run for years and accept proposals on a rolling basis. DARPA typically funds research and development, not production.

2. **Other Transactions (OTs):** DARPA was one of the original users of OT authority, which allows it to award contracts outside the FAR. OTs are faster, more flexible, and more accessible to commercial companies that don't have government accounting infrastructure.

DARPA funding is almost always RDT&E money—research funding. DARPA does not buy production systems. It funds technology development and demonstration. If your DARPA-funded technology succeeds, the transition to production happens through a different organization—a service acquisition office, a PEO/PAE, or a special program. This transition from DARPA research to military production is one of the most notorious failure points in the defense innovation ecosystem (the "Valley of Death" described in Chapter 12).

**DARPA's budget was slated for a 6.1% increase in FY2025**, even as other basic and applied research accounts faced cuts—a signal that the DoD values DARPA's high-risk, high-reward approach even during budget-constrained periods.

## DIU: The Bridge Between Silicon Valley and the Pentagon

The Defense Innovation Unit was created in 2015 (originally as DIUx, the "x" was eventually dropped) to bridge the gap between the commercial technology sector and the military. DIU is headquartered in Mountain View, California—literally in Silicon Valley—with additional offices in Boston, Austin, and the Pentagon.

### The Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO)

DIU's primary mechanism is the **Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO)**, a procurement process that uses Other Transaction (OT) authority to rapidly prototype and field commercial dual-use technologies.

**How the CSO process works:**

1. **Problem Statement:** DIU identifies a military problem and publishes an Area of Interest on its website describing the capability gap.
2. **Submission:** Companies submit solution briefs—short (typically 5-10 page) descriptions of their technology and how it addresses the problem. There is no traditional government proposal with 200 pages of compliance documentation. The submission is streamlined and designed to be accessible to commercial companies.
3. **Evaluation:** DIU evaluates submissions in weeks, not months. The agency aims for prototype agreement awards in **60-90 days** from submission, though the average is closer to 120 days. Compare this to the 6-18 months a traditional FAR-based contract can take.
4. **Prototype Agreement:** If selected, DIU awards an OT prototype agreement—typically $1-5 million—to demonstrate the technology in a military context.
5. **Transition:** If the prototype succeeds, DIU facilitates a **follow-on production OT** or a FAR-based contract with a military customer. Critically, successful prototypes can transition to production **without a new competition**—the follow-on production contract can be awarded sole-source to the company that demonstrated the prototype.

### DIU's Track Record

DIU has awarded **over 270 prototype agreements** through CSOs. Of these, approximately **41% have successfully transitioned** to fielded technologies through follow-on production contracts or service agreements. This transition rate is remarkable for the defense world—the military's historical track record for transitioning prototypes to production is far worse.

In raw numbers, DIU transitioned 35 prototypes into production between FY2016 and FY2022, with 17 transitions in FY2022 alone. The agency's focus areas include AI and machine learning, autonomy, cyber, energy, human systems, and space.

### DIU 3.0

Launched in late 2023, DIU's current strategy—**DIU 3.0**—emphasizes delivering technology at scale rather than just proving concepts. The strategy focuses on:

- Expanding DIU's CSO process to a broader range of DoD users
- Accelerating the transition from prototype to production
- Supporting the March 2025 SecDef memorandum's mandate to use CSOs and OTAs as default contracting mechanisms for software

The March 2025 memorandum is particularly important for DIU because it essentially elevated DIU's approach—commercial-first acquisition using OTAs and CSOs—from an alternative pathway to the **default method** for software acquisition across the entire DoD. DIU's model is now being replicated and scaled Department-wide.

**Why DIU matters for startups:**

DIU is designed for you. Its CSO process doesn't require FAR compliance, cost accounting systems, or traditional government past performance. The submission is short, the evaluation is fast, and a successful prototype can lead directly to a production contract without re-competing. If your technology has dual-use potential—meaning it has both commercial and military applications—DIU is one of the best entry points into the defense market.

## Service-Level Innovation Organizations

Beyond DIU, each military service has its own innovation organization designed to lower barriers for commercial and nontraditional companies.

### AFWERX (Air Force)

AFWERX is the most active service-level innovation unit. Its three main pillars—AFVentures, Spark, and Prime—cover the full innovation lifecycle from ideation through production. AFVentures manages the Air Force's SBIR/STTR programs, including the revolutionary **Open Topic** SBIR, which allows companies to pitch ideas that address Air Force needs without matching a specific government-defined topic. AFWERX has awarded thousands of SBIR contracts and processed billions in funding, making it the largest single source of SBIR activity in the DoD.

### Army Applications Lab (AAL)

AAL operates as the Army's bridge to the innovation ecosystem. It uses OTAs, prize competitions, and venture-style engagement to connect commercial technologies with Army modernization priorities. AAL has a specific mandate to engage nontraditional defense companies—companies that haven't worked with the military before—and it actively recruits startups through pitch events and technology demonstrations.

### NavalX

NavalX serves as the Navy and Marine Corps' innovation organization, running tech bridges across the country that connect regional innovation ecosystems with naval requirements. NavalX's approach is more distributed than AFWERX or AAL—it emphasizes building connections between Navy problem-owners and local industry clusters rather than running a centralized funding program.

### SpaceWERX

The Space Force's innovation arm, modeled after AFWERX. SpaceWERX runs SBIR/STTR programs, Pitch Days, and Tactical Funding Increases (TACFIs) specifically for space-related technologies. The commercial space industry overlap makes SpaceWERX particularly relevant for companies already in the commercial space sector.

**The bottom line on service innovation organizations:** These are your friendliest entry points. They were created specifically to make it easier for nontraditional companies to work with the military. They use simplified contracting mechanisms, shorter timelines, and entrepreneurial engagement models. Start here.

## DISA: The Gatekeeper of Military Networks and Cloud

The Defense Information Systems Agency is not a household name, but for software companies selling to the military, DISA may be the most consequential organization you'll interact with.

### What DISA Does

DISA provides, operates, and assures the DoD's global communications and computing infrastructure. This includes:

- **NIPRNET:** The DoD's unclassified but operationally significant network
- **SIPRNET:** The DoD's SECRET-level classified network
- **JWICS:** The Joint Worldwide Intelligence Communications System (TOP SECRET/SCI)
- **Cloud authorization and security requirements** for all DoD cloud services

If your software needs to run on a DoD network, DISA's security requirements apply to you. If your software is delivered as a cloud service (SaaS), DISA's cloud authorization framework determines whether and how you can operate.

### The DoD Cloud Computing Security Requirements Guide (CC SRG)

The CC SRG is the document that defines what security level your cloud offering must meet to host different types of DoD data. It establishes **Impact Levels (ILs)** that correspond to the sensitivity of the data:

| Impact Level | Data Type | Authorization Basis | What It Means in Practice |
|---|---|---|---|
| **IL2** | Public or non-critical mission data | FedRAMP Moderate | The entry point. Your software can host publicly releasable DoD data. Many commercial SaaS products start here. |
| **IL4** | Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) | FedRAMP Moderate + DoD-specific controls | This is where most mission-relevant unclassified data lives. Getting to IL4 is the minimum threshold for handling sensitive but unclassified DoD information. |
| **IL5** | Higher-sensitivity CUI, National Security Systems | FedRAMP High + ~175 additional controls | This is the threshold for mission-critical applications. As of 2025, IL5 is explicitly categorized as National Security System information. Getting to IL5 is expensive and time-consuming but opens the door to the most valuable government data. |
| **IL6** | Classified information up to SECRET | Classified environment requirements | The highest current cloud impact level. Requires dedicated infrastructure in accredited facilities. As of early 2025, all four JWCC providers (AWS, Google, Microsoft, Oracle) have achieved or are approaching IL6 authorization. |

**Key 2025 changes:** The SRG was split into two documents (one for Cloud Service Providers, one for Mission Owners). New reciprocity guidelines between FedRAMP baselines and DoD Impact Levels were established. CSPs with existing IL5/IL6 ATOs must update to NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 by end of CY2025.

### The Joint Warfighting Cloud Capability (JWCC)

JWCC is the DoD's multi-vendor, multi-cloud contract—the successor to the canceled JEDI contract. JWCC provides the DoD with access to commercial cloud services from **Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, and Oracle** across all classification levels, from unclassified through SECRET.

**Why JWCC matters for software startups:**

You probably won't be a JWCC provider (those contracts went to the hyperscale cloud companies). But JWCC affects you because it determines the cloud infrastructure your software will run on. If your application is built for AWS and a DoD customer's environment runs on Azure through JWCC, you need to be cloud-agnostic—or at least deployable to the customer's JWCC environment.

JWCC also provides a procurement pathway that simplifies cloud purchasing for DoD customers. A government buyer who wants to run your SaaS application on DoD-authorized cloud infrastructure can use JWCC task orders to provision the underlying cloud resources, which may simplify the overall acquisition for your product.

### DISA's Cloud Authorization Process

To deploy your cloud service offering in a DoD environment, you must obtain a **DoD Provisional Authorization (PA)** from DISA. This process requires:

1. Having a **FedRAMP authorization** at the appropriate level (Moderate for IL2/IL4, High for IL5)
2. Meeting additional DoD-specific security requirements ("FedRAMP+")
3. Being assessed by a Third-Party Assessment Organization (3PAO)
4. Having DISA validate the assessment results and issue the PA

This process is expensive, time-consuming (18-24+ months for initial authorization), and ongoing (annual reassessments required). But it is the price of admission for cloud-based software in the DoD. We'll cover this process in excruciating detail in Chapter 8.

## DoD Software Factories: The Government's In-House Development Shops

DoD Software Factories are government-run software development organizations that use modern commercial practices—Agile, DevSecOps, cloud-native architectures—to build and deliver military software applications. They represent the DoD's attempt to develop internal software competency rather than relying entirely on contractors.

### The Major Software Factories

**Platform One (Air Force)**

The most mature and widely used DoD software factory. Platform One isn't just a development shop—it's a DevSecOps platform-as-a-service that other organizations across the DoD can use. Its key offerings include:
- **Iron Bank:** Hardened container repository (discussed above)
- **Party Bus:** Managed CI/CD pipeline
- **Big Bang:** Kubernetes-based deployment platform

Platform One is actively seeking commercial partnerships. It runs SBIR Pitch Days and solicits proposals for innovative, dual-use tools that can enhance its infrastructure.

**Kessel Run (Air Force)**

Originally created to modernize the Air Operations Center (AOC) software, Kessel Run proved that military software could be built with the same speed and agility as commercial software. It was the proof-of-concept that inspired the broader software factory movement.

**Black Pearl (Navy)**

The Navy's DevSecOps platform, providing continuous software delivery capabilities for naval applications. Black Pearl operates within the DoD's Software Modernization Strategy framework and coordinates with other software factories.

**Army Software Factory**

The newest major software factory, the Army Software Factory takes a unique approach: it trains active-duty soldiers to be software engineers. Soldiers apply to the program, undergo intensive training in modern software development, and then build applications that address Army operational needs. The ASWF is actively engaging commercial vendors for education platforms, hybrid cloud infrastructure, and cybersecurity tools.

### How Software Factories Interact with Commercial Vendors

This relationship is nuanced. Software factories are, in some respects, your competitors—they can build software internally rather than buying it from you. But they're also potential customers and partners:

- **Tooling:** Software factories need commercial tools—IDEs, testing frameworks, monitoring tools, CI/CD components. If you build developer tools or infrastructure software, software factories are customers.
- **Platform deployment:** If your application can deploy on Platform One's infrastructure or run containers from Iron Bank, you have a much easier path to adoption across the DoD.
- **Integration:** Even when software factories build applications internally, they often need to integrate commercial products for specialized capabilities—AI/ML models, data analytics, cybersecurity tools.
- **Capability gaps:** Software factories can't build everything. When they identify a capability gap that commercial products already fill, they look to vendors.

The DoD Software Modernization Implementation Plan for FY25-26 explicitly calls for expanding and replicating the software factory ecosystem, establishing a "commercial-first" approach, and embedding DevSecOps as a standard practice. This is good news for commercial software companies whose products align with the software factory philosophy of continuous delivery, cloud-native architecture, and DevSecOps integration.

## The Intelligence Community: A Separate Universe (That Shares an Orbit)

The Intelligence Community (IC) is technically separate from the DoD, but the two are deeply intertwined. Several IC agencies are part of the DoD, and the IC's budget—approximately $100 billion annually across its 18 member organizations—represents another massive market for technology companies.

### Key IC Organizations

**Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)**

The CIA is the U.S.'s primary foreign intelligence agency. Its technology and procurement needs are managed internally, and its acquisition processes are highly classified and opaque. The CIA has its own CIO, its own development shops, and its own (classified) cloud infrastructure.

**National Security Agency (NSA)**

NSA handles signals intelligence (SIGINT) and cybersecurity. It is one of the world's largest employers of mathematicians and computer scientists. NSA's cybersecurity mission—through the Cybersecurity Directorate—produces guidelines and tools that affect the entire federal government, including the CNSA 2.0 algorithm suite for post-quantum cryptography.

**National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA)**

NGA processes and analyzes geospatial intelligence—satellite imagery, mapping data, and geodetic information. NGA is a major consumer of AI/ML technology for image analysis, change detection, and pattern recognition.

**National Reconnaissance Office (NRO)**

NRO designs, builds, and operates the U.S.'s intelligence satellites. It operates with an enormous budget and works closely with the commercial space industry.

### In-Q-Tel: The IC's Venture Arm

**In-Q-Tel (IQT)** is a nonprofit venture capital firm that invests in commercial technology companies on behalf of the Intelligence Community. Created in 1999, IQT identifies emerging technologies that could benefit the IC, makes strategic investments in companies developing those technologies, and helps bridge the gap between Silicon Valley innovation and intelligence community needs.

IQT's investment does not come with strings attached in the traditional defense contractor sense—they invest in commercial companies and help adapt commercial products for intelligence applications. An IQT investment is a strong signal that your technology has intelligence community relevance, and it opens doors across the IC and, increasingly, the DoD.

IQT focuses on areas including: AI/ML, biotechnology, cybersecurity, data analytics, autonomous systems, quantum computing, and advanced materials. If the CIA needs a capability that the commercial sector is developing, IQT is how they find and fund it.

## Combatant Commands: Where Requirements Are Born

There are eleven **Combatant Commands (COCOMs)**, each responsible for a geographic region or a functional area. COCOMs are the operational commands—the organizations that actually plan and conduct military operations. They're led by four-star generals or admirals and report directly to the Secretary of Defense and the President.

### Why COCOMs Matter for Founders

COCOMs are where operational requirements are *generated*. When a Geographic Combatant Commander identifies a capability gap—"we need a better way to track adversary naval movements in the Pacific"—that requirement flows into the JCIDS process and eventually drives acquisition decisions.

**Geographic Combatant Commands:**

| Command | Area of Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **U.S. Indo-Pacific Command (INDOPACOM)** | Pacific and Indian Ocean regions. The top priority for China-related capabilities. |
| **U.S. European Command (EUCOM)** | Europe, including Russia deterrence. Primary customer for NATO interoperability. |
| **U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM)** | Middle East, including ongoing operations and counterterrorism. |
| **U.S. Africa Command (AFRICOM)** | Africa. Growing importance for counterterrorism and Chinese competition. |
| **U.S. Southern Command (SOUTHCOM)** | Central and South America, Caribbean. |
| **U.S. Northern Command (NORTHCOM)** | North America, including homeland defense. |

**Functional Combatant Commands:**

| Command | Function |
|---|---|
| **U.S. Special Operations Command (SOCOM)** | Special operations forces across all services. SOCOM has its own acquisition authority and is often one of the fastest-moving organizations for technology adoption. |
| **U.S. Cyber Command (CYBERCOM)** | Cyberspace operations, including offensive and defensive cyber. Critical customer for cybersecurity companies. |
| **U.S. Strategic Command (STRATCOM)** | Nuclear deterrence, space operations, missile defense. |
| **U.S. Transportation Command (TRANSCOM)** | Global logistics and transportation. |
| **U.S. Space Command (SPACECOM)** | Space domain operations. |

**SOCOM is particularly important for startups.** It has its own acquisition authority (separate from the services), a culture that prizes agility and innovation, and a track record of adopting commercial technology quickly. SOCOM's Acquisition, Technology, and Logistics (AT&L) office and its SOFWERX innovation lab are both startup-friendly entry points.

## The Oversight and Support Agencies

These agencies don't buy things or generate requirements, but they can accelerate or derail your business depending on how well you understand and comply with their requirements.

### DCSA: Defense Counterintelligence and Security Agency

DCSA is responsible for:
- **Personnel security clearances:** Processing and adjudicating security clearance investigations for military, civilian, and contractor personnel
- **Facility security clearances:** Issuing Facility Clearance Letters (FCLs) that authorize your company to access classified information
- **Industrial security oversight:** Ensuring that cleared contractor facilities meet security requirements
- **CMMC assessments:** As of the CMMC final rule (effective December 2024), DCSA manages the CMMC ecosystem, including accrediting third-party assessment organizations (C3PAOs)

If you need to work on classified programs or handle Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), you will interact with DCSA. The clearance process is notoriously slow—even with reforms, initial Secret clearances can take 3-6 months and Top Secret clearances can take 12+ months. Planning for clearance timelines is essential.

### DCAA: Defense Contract Audit Agency

DCAA audits defense contractors' financial systems, accounting practices, incurred costs, and pricing proposals. If you have a cost-reimbursement contract (cost-plus), DCAA will audit your accounting system to verify that you're charging only allowable costs and that your indirect rates (overhead, G&A, fringe) are calculated correctly.

**For startups, DCAA is important because:**
- A DCAA-adequate accounting system is required for cost-reimbursement contracts
- Building this system (compliant timekeeping, cost segregation, indirect rate calculations, unallowable cost tracking) costs $50,000-$150,000+ and takes months
- Your first contract may require a DCAA pre-award audit before the government will agree to a cost-plus structure
- Getting this wrong can result in disallowed costs, withheld payments, or—in extreme cases—fraud investigations

You can avoid DCAA by sticking to FFP or T&M contracts, but many R&D contracts are cost-plus, and avoiding them limits your market.

### DCMA: Defense Contract Management Agency

DCMA administers contracts after they're awarded. This means:
- Monitoring contractor performance
- Approving progress payments
- Conducting quality assurance inspections
- Accepting deliverables on behalf of the government

Your interaction with DCMA will depend on the type and size of your contract. For smaller contracts, DCMA's involvement may be minimal. For larger or more complex programs, a DCMA Administrative Contracting Officer (ACO) will be assigned to your company and will have significant influence over payment timing, change order processing, and performance evaluations.

## The Small Business Administration (SBA) and Set-Aside Programs

The federal government is legally required to direct a certain percentage of its contracting dollars to small businesses. The SBA oversees this system through a network of programs, certifications, and set-asides.

### Small Business Size Standards

The first question is whether you qualify as a "small business." The SBA defines size standards by NAICS code (North American Industry Classification System). For most software and IT services companies, the size standard is based on annual revenue—typically **$34 million** to **$40.5 million** in average annual receipts over three years, depending on the specific NAICS code. For some manufacturing codes, the standard is based on employee count.

If your company is below the size standard for your NAICS code, you're eligible for small business set-aside contracts.

### Small Business Set-Aside Programs

The government has a **23% overall goal** for small business contracting, with sub-goals for specific categories:

| Program | Eligibility | Government Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Small Business (SB)** | Below SBA size standard for your NAICS code | 23% overall |
| **Small Disadvantaged Business (SDB)** | Small business owned by socially/economically disadvantaged individuals | 5% |
| **Women-Owned Small Business (WOSB)** | 51%+ owned and controlled by women | 5% |
| **Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business (SDVOSB)** | 51%+ owned by service-disabled veterans | 3% |
| **HUBZone** | Small business with principal office in a Historically Underutilized Business Zone | 3% |
| **8(a) Business Development Program** | Small disadvantaged businesses; provides a 9-year developmental program including sole-source contract eligibility | N/A |

**The 8(a) program deserves special attention** because 8(a) companies can receive sole-source contracts up to $4.5 million (for services) or $7 million (for manufacturing) without competition. This is an extraordinarily powerful tool for small businesses that qualify. The 8(a) program also provides mentoring, training, and access to federal contracting opportunities that are not available to non-8(a) companies.

### Why Set-Asides Matter for Startups

Set-aside programs create a protected competitive space where you're not competing against Lockheed Martin and Raytheon. A contract that is "set aside" for small businesses can only be bid on by companies that meet the small business size standard. This dramatically levels the playing field.

Additionally, many large prime contractors need small business subcontractors to meet their own small business subcontracting goals. This creates teaming opportunities where you can work as a subcontractor to a large prime—gaining experience, past performance, and revenue—while the prime handles the complex compliance requirements.

## The Mental Map: Putting It All Together

You've now been introduced to several dozen organizations. Here's how to think about them in terms of what *you* need:

### Who Has the Money?

Money flows from Congress through PPBE to the military services and defense agencies. The people who actually spend that money are:
- **PAEs/PEOs and their Program Managers** — They control program budgets and issue contracts
- **DARPA Program Managers** — They fund research and advanced technology development
- **DIU** — They fund prototypes through CSOs
- **Service innovation organizations** (AFWERX, AAL, NavalX, SpaceWERX) — They fund SBIRs and small-scale demonstrations
- **COCOMs** (especially SOCOM) — Some have their own acquisition authority and funding

### Who Sets the Rules?

- **USD(A&S)** — Acquisition policy, including the AAF, Software Acquisition Pathway, and PAE mandate
- **DoD CIO** — Cybersecurity policy, CMMC, cloud standards
- **CDAO** — AI/data policy, software modernization priorities
- **DISA** — Cloud security requirements (CC SRG), network standards
- **DCSA** — Security clearances and industrial security
- **DCAA** — Accounting system standards and audit requirements

### Who Can Help You Get In?

- **DIU** — CSO process, OTA authority, Silicon Valley presence
- **AFWERX / SpaceWERX** — SBIR/STTR, Open Topic, pitch events
- **AAL** — Army-specific OTA engagement
- **NavalX** — Naval tech bridges and innovation partnerships
- **In-Q-Tel** — IC venture capital investment
- **SOCOM SOFWERX** — Special operations innovation lab
- **Platform One / Iron Bank** — Software factory engagement for containerized applications

### Who Can Block You?

- **DCAA** — Audit findings on your accounting system
- **DCSA** — Clearance delays, facility clearance denials
- **DISA** — Cloud authorization delays, ATO requirements
- **Contracting Officers** — Every contract must go through a warranted Contracting Officer, who has enormous power to approve, modify, or reject contract actions

## Chapter Summary

The Department of Defense is not one buyer—it's an ecosystem of hundreds of organizations, each with its own budget, authorities, and culture. The five military services (Army, Navy, Air Force, Marine Corps, Space Force) each operate their own acquisition infrastructure, now transitioning from the PEO model to Portfolio Acquisition Executives under the FoRGED Act. OSD offices—particularly USD(A&S), USD(R&E), CDAO, and the DoD CIO—set the policies and priorities that govern how the entire system operates.

For software companies, the most important organizations include the CDAO (which drives AI and software modernization policy with a $139.9M AI/ML budget, now under USD(R&E) as of August 2025), the DoD Software Factories (Platform One, Iron Bank, Kessel Run, Black Pearl, Army Software Factory), and DISA (which controls the cloud authorization framework through Impact Levels IL2-IL6 and the JWCC contract).

Innovation entry points—DIU's Commercial Solutions Opening (41% transition rate, 270+ prototype agreements), AFWERX, AAL, NavalX, SpaceWERX, and In-Q-Tel—were designed specifically for nontraditional companies. Support and oversight agencies—DCSA (clearances), DCAA (financial audits), and DCMA (contract administration)—set compliance requirements that can accelerate or derail your business.

The key to navigating this ecosystem is understanding who has money, who sets rules, who can help you in, and who can block you. The PAE who controls your capability area's requirements, budget, and acquisition is your most important relationship. The innovation organizations are your most accessible entry point. The oversight agencies are the compliance gauntlets you must prepare for. And the CDAO, DISA, and the software factories determine the software-specific landscape that will shape your path to production.

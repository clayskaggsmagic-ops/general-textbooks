# Selling to the Military — Chapter Research Prompts

These prompts are designed to guide comprehensive, up-to-date research for each chapter of the textbook. Each prompt tells the writer what to investigate, what context to provide, and what the reader should walk away understanding. The field of defense contracting changes rapidly—regulations are updated, new authorities are created, and the political landscape shifts constantly—so each prompt emphasizes pulling current information.

---

## Chapter 1: The Landscape — How the Department of Defense Actually Buys Things

**Research Prompt:**

Research and explain the entire DoD acquisition system from the perspective of an outsider trying to understand how the largest buyer on Earth actually purchases things. Cover:

- The scale of DoD spending (annual budget, how it breaks down between services, agencies, and components)
- The three decision support systems: PPBE (Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution), JCIDS (Joint Capabilities Integration and Development System), and the Defense Acquisition System
- The acquisition lifecycle phases: Materiel Solution Analysis → Technology Maturation & Risk Reduction → Engineering & Manufacturing Development → Production & Deployment → Operations & Support
- What "milestones" are and how the Defense Acquisition Board (DAB) acts as a gatekeeper
- ACAT categories (Acquisition Category I, II, III) and why they matter for the size and oversight of programs
- The difference between a "program of record" and everything else (pilot projects, experiments, prototypes)
- How requirements flow from the warfighter through JCIDS to actual solicitations
- The major contract types: Firm-Fixed-Price (FFP), Cost-Plus (Cost-Reimbursement), Time & Materials (T&M), and Indefinite Delivery/Indefinite Quantity (IDIQ)
- **The Software Acquisition Pathway (DoDI 5000.87)** — the dedicated acquisition pathway for software-intensive systems, how it differs from traditional hardware-centric acquisition, the March 2025 SecDef memorandum mandating its use as the preferred method for all software components, and why this is a game-changer for software startups
- The Adaptive Acquisition Framework's six pathways and why the Software Acquisition Pathway and Middle Tier Acquisition are the most relevant for software companies
- Why the system is slow and what reform efforts have been attempted

The reader should finish this chapter understanding the basic architecture of how defense buying works—the pipeline from "the military needs something" to "a contractor gets paid to deliver it." This is foundational context for everything else in the book.

- **DoD Software Factories** — Platform One (Air Force DevSecOps platform), Iron Bank (hardened container repository), Kessel Run (Air Force), Black Pearl (Navy), Army Software Factory, and how these government-run software development organizations interact with commercial vendors

## Chapter 3: The Intelligence Community — Selling to the Other Side of National Security

**Research Prompt:**

Research how the civilian Intelligence Community (IC) buys technology—a world that operates parallel to but distinctly from the Department of Defense, with its own organizations, funding mechanisms, procurement processes, and culture of secrecy. Cover:

- The 18 agencies of the Intelligence Community—CIA, NSA, NGA, NRO, DIA, FBI (intelligence division), and others—and what each one actually does at a high level
- The Office of the Director of National Intelligence (ODNI)—its coordinating role, how it sets IC-wide strategy (IC Data Strategy, National Intelligence Strategy), and its authority over cross-agency initiatives
- How the IC budget works—the National Intelligence Program (NIP) vs. the Military Intelligence Program (MIP), the classified "black budget," why IC spending totals are classified but top-line numbers are released, and how IC funding flows differently than DoD procurement funding
- The Congressional oversight structure for intelligence—the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence (SSCI) and the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence (HPSCI), and how they differ from HASC/SASC
- In-Q-Tel (IQT)—the IC's non-profit strategic venture arm: how it works, its investment model (equity + technology development agreements), its focus areas (AI, cybersecurity, biotech, advanced materials, critical minerals), how it scouts and evaluates startups, and how an IQT investment can open doors across the entire IC
- The proposed "Intelligence Community Technology Bridge Fund"—efforts to help small businesses with IC ties transition from R&D to production
- IARPA (Intelligence Advanced Research Projects Activity)—its role as the IC's equivalent of DARPA, how it funds high-risk/high-payoff research, its Broad Agency Announcements and proposers' days, and how IARPA research transitions to IC customers
- CIA's evolving acquisition framework—the 2025 procurement reforms, the streamlined vendor vetting system, the "Digital Gateway" industry engagement platform, and the agency's push into AI, biotech, and microelectronics
- NSA's Cybersecurity Directorate and how it engages with commercial cybersecurity companies
- NGA and NRO—how geospatial intelligence and overhead reconnaissance create opportunities for companies in satellite imagery, remote sensing, and geospatial AI
- How IC contracting differs from DoD contracting—smaller contract values but higher classification requirements, emphasis on sole-source and limited competition, the role of contract vehicles like Alliant 2 and CIO-SP4
- The cultural differences between selling to the IC vs. DoD—IC agencies are smaller, more mission-focused, faster to adopt but harder to find; the relationship-driven nature of IC procurement
- Classification as a barrier and a moat—why IC work almost always requires TS/SCI clearances, and how the classified environment creates long-term competitive advantages for companies that get in
- The IC's approach to open-source intelligence (OSINT) and commercially available information (CAI)—the emerging IC Data Consortium and how startups providing commercial data products can sell to the IC without classified infrastructure
- Secondment programs and talent exchange—how the IC is sending analysts to work at tech companies and inviting tech talent into agencies

The reader should finish this chapter understanding that the Intelligence Community is a distinct customer from the military, with its own budget, its own procurement culture, and its own entry points—and that for certain types of technology (AI, cyber, data analytics, OSINT), the IC can be a faster and more accessible customer than DoD.

- The Truth in Negotiations Act and cost or pricing data requirements
- The Federal Anti-Kickback Act, the False Claims Act, and the Procurement Integrity Act—the laws that send people to prison
- Bid protests—what they are, how GAO and the Court of Federal Claims handle them, and what they mean for competitors
- Compliance requirements that flow down to subcontractors
- How contract disputes work (the Contract Disputes Act, contracting officer final decisions, Board of Contract Appeals)
- Ethics and compliance programs—mandatory disclosure rules, suspension, and debarment
- **The Secure Software Development Framework (SSDF)** — NIST guidance on secure software development practices that federal agencies now require software producers to attest to, the FY2023 NDAA requirement that DoD only acquire software free from known vulnerabilities, and supply chain security obligations

## Chapter 8: Cybersecurity and Compliance — CMMC, ATO, DevSecOps, and the Software Security Gauntlet

**Research Prompt:**

Research the cybersecurity compliance requirements that have become the single biggest barrier to entry for defense software startups—covering both the organizational compliance (CMMC) and the product-level compliance (ATO) that software companies must navigate. This is arguably the most important chapter for a software startup. Cover:

**Organizational Cybersecurity Compliance:**
- Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI)—what it is, how it's designated, and why its protection is mandatory in defense contracting
- NIST SP 800-171—the 110 security controls that every company handling CUI must implement; explain each family of controls at a high level
- CMMC 2.0 (Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification)—the three levels, what each requires, the timeline for phased rollout in DoD contracts (starting 2025-2026), and how CMMC differs from self-attestation under DFARS 252.204-7012
- The CMMC assessment process—who performs assessments (C3PAOs), what it costs, how long it takes, and what happens if you fail
- SPRS scores (Supplier Performance Risk System)—what they are, how they're calculated, and why they're checked before contract award
- Plans of Action and Milestones (POA&Ms)—how many you're allowed to have and still be certified
- The enclave/boundary strategy—how startups can limit the scope (and cost) of their CUI environment rather than securing their entire network

**Product-Level Cybersecurity — The ATO Process (Critical for Software Companies):**
- **Authority to Operate (ATO)** — the formal security certification that grants your software the ability to run within a DoD environment; why no software deploys to DoD systems without one; what an Authorizing Official (AO) is and how they make risk acceptance decisions
- **The NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)** — the seven-step process (Prepare → Categorize → Select → Implement → Assess → Authorize → Monitor) that governs all ATO decisions; NIST SP 800-37, NIST SP 800-53, and FIPS 199
- **Why ATO is the #1 killer of software deals** — the traditional ATO process takes 9-18 months, costs $500K-$2M+ in documentation and assessment, and produces a point-in-time certification that becomes stale with every code update
- **Continuous ATO (cATO)** — the 2022 DoD memorandum establishing cATO as the modernized alternative; how cATO shifts from point-in-time snapshots to continuous risk monitoring; the requirements for achieving cATO (continuous monitoring, active cyber defense, secure software supply chain); and why cATO is transformative for software companies that can achieve it
- **The DevSecOps pipeline as a cATO prerequisite** — what a compliant DevSecOps pipeline looks like: automated security testing in CI/CD (SAST, DAST, container scanning), policy-as-code enforcement of NIST 800-53 controls, automated evidence collection, real-time compliance dashboards, and Infrastructure as Code (IaC) compliance gates
- **DoD Software Factories and how to use them** — Platform One (Air Force), Iron Bank (hardened container repository), Black Pearl (Navy), Army Software Factory; how deploying through a software factory with an existing ATO can dramatically accelerate your own ATO timeline; the difference between getting your containers into Iron Bank vs. building your own ATO from scratch
- **FIPS 140-2/140-3 cryptographic requirements** — when your software must use FIPS-validated encryption modules, what this means in practice, and how to implement it without breaking your application

**Cloud and SaaS-Specific Compliance:**
- FedRAMP (Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program)—what it is, the different authorization levels, whether your SaaS product needs it to sell to DoD, the cost and timeline (24+ months, millions annually to maintain), and alternatives like FedRAMP Tailored/Low
- **DoD Cloud Computing SRG (Security Requirements Guide)** — Impact Levels explained in detail:
  - **IL2:** Public/non-critical data, aligns with FedRAMP Moderate baseline
  - **IL4:** Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), FedRAMP Moderate + DoD-specific enhancements
  - **IL5:** Mission-critical CUI, dedicated infrastructure, U.S.-citizen-only personnel, 421+ security controls
  - **IL6:** Classified (SECRET level), dedicated cloud enclave on SIPRNet
  - How to determine which Impact Level your software needs, and why most software startups should target IL4 or IL5
- **Joint Warfighting Cloud Capability (JWCC)** — the multi-cloud contract (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, Oracle) and how your software's cloud hosting must connect to JWCC-authorized cloud environments
- **The "FedRAMP or equivalent" question** — when DFARS 252.204-7012 requires FedRAMP Moderate equivalence vs. full FedRAMP authorization, and how to navigate this with your contracting officer

**Secure Software Development:**
- **The Secure Software Development Framework (SSDF)** — NIST guidance that federal agencies now require software producers to attest to; the FY2023 NDAA requirement that DoD only acquire software free from known vulnerabilities; and how this changes your development practices
- **Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)** — what it is, why the government increasingly requires one, and how to generate and maintain SBOMs for your software supply chain
- **Open-source software in government** — the DoD's stance on open-source components, how to manage open-source risk, and the Iron Bank model for vetting and hardening open-source containers

**Practical Guidance:**
- Common mistakes software startups make: underestimating ATO timelines, treating CMMC and ATO as the same thing (they're not), failing to architect for compliance from day one, not separating CUI environments, and trying to get FedRAMP when a DoD-specific ATO would suffice
- The cost of compliance and how to budget for it realistically ($50K-$500K+ for CMMC depending on scope; $500K-$2M+ for a traditional ATO; significantly less if you leverage a software factory)
- A phased roadmap for software startups: when to focus on CMMC vs. ATO vs. FedRAMP based on your stage and target customer

The reader should finish this chapter with a clear understanding that software companies face TWO parallel compliance burdens (organizational via CMMC and product-level via ATO), a realistic assessment of the cost and timeline for each, and a strategy for building compliance incrementally with a focus on DevSecOps and cATO as the path that aligns with modern software development.

- Data rights negotiations—what's negotiable, what's not, and when to push back

- Why IP protection is often the #1 concern for defense tech VCs when evaluating portfolio companies

This is the chapter most founders skip—and the one that explains why their technology sits unfunded despite military enthusiasm. Cover:

- The constitutional foundation—Article I, Section 9 gives Congress the "power of the purse," meaning *nothing* gets bought by the Pentagon without congressional authorization and appropriation
- The two-step legislative process: Authorization (setting policy and spending ceilings) vs. Appropriation (actually providing the money)—and why both must happen for your program to be funded
- The National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA)—the annual must-pass bill that has been enacted for 60+ consecutive years, how it works, its timeline (President's budget request → committee hearings → markup → floor votes → conference committee → signed into law), and why provisions inserted into the NDAA can create or destroy entire programs
- The four key committees:
  - **Senate Armed Services Committee (SASC)**—membership, subcommittee structure, and its role in shaping defense policy
  - **House Armed Services Committee (HASC)**—same, and how it differs from SASC in priorities and approach
  - **Senate Appropriations Committee (Defense subcommittee)**—who actually writes the checks
  - **House Appropriations Committee (Defense subcommittee)**—same for the House side
- The intelligence oversight committees—SSCI and HPSCI—and how they control IC funding separately from defense spending
- How defense budgets are structured—the major appropriation titles (Military Personnel, O&M, Procurement, RDT&E, Military Construction) and what each one funds
- The "color of money" problem in legislative context—why a program authorized under RDT&E can't simply shift to Procurement without congressional action
- Continuing Resolutions (CRs)—what happens when Congress fails to pass appropriations bills on time, why CRs freeze spending at prior-year levels, and how this kills new program starts
- Government shutdowns—how they affect defense contracting, which contracts continue and which stop, and the ripple effects on startups with small cash reserves
- Congressional earmarks and congressionally directed spending—how members of Congress insert specific funding for specific programs, and how this can benefit (or bypass) startups
- The role of congressional staffers—why the 25-year-old professional staff members on HASC and SASC often have more practical influence over defense policy than the senators and representatives they work for
- How to engage Congress legally:
  - What lobbying actually is—the legal definition, registration requirements under the Lobbying Disclosure Act, and when your congressional engagement crosses the line from "education" to "lobbying"
  - Whether a startup should hire a lobbyist—the cost ($5K-$25K+/month), what they actually do, when it's worth it, and how to find one who specializes in defense tech
  - Government affairs vs. lobbying—what a GR team does vs. a registered lobbyist
  - Congressional testimony and hearing participation—how to get invited, how to prepare, and why being asked to testify is a massive credibility signal
  - The role of the Congressional Research Service (CRS) and GAO reports in shaping congressional views on defense technology
- The "revolving door" between Congress and industry—how former staffers become defense consultants and lobbyists, and the legal restrictions (one-year cooling-off for staff, two-year for members)
- How the NDAA creates opportunities for startups—recent provisions expanding OTA authority, SBIR reforms, CMMC requirements, and acquisition streamlining that were all driven by congressional action
- The political dynamics of defense spending—why defense budgets enjoy bipartisan support (for now), how geopolitical events drive emergency supplemental appropriations, and how the politics of specific weapons systems or technology areas create windows of opportunity
- Defense lobbying by established contractors—$100M+ in annual lobbying spending by the Big Five primes, 500+ former government officials working as defense lobbyists, and how this creates an uneven playing field for startups
- Practical strategies for startups: building relationships with district offices, leveraging your facility's location for jobs-based arguments, participating in congressional caucuses (e.g., the Defense Innovation Caucus), and using trade associations (NDIA, NSIN alumni networks) as force multipliers

The reader should finish this chapter understanding that Congress is not a spectator in defense procurement—it is the ultimate decision-maker on what gets funded, and that engaging with the legislative process is not optional for any defense tech company that wants to survive beyond the prototype stage.

## Chapter 16: Staying Alive — Compliance, Ethics, and the Long-Term Game

**Research Prompt:**

Research the ongoing compliance obligations and ethical pitfalls that defense contractors face after they've won their first contract—the things that can get established companies debarred, their executives imprisoned, or their contracts terminated. Cover:

- Mandatory disclosure rules—the obligation to report overpayments, cost mischarges, fraud, and violations of federal criminal law to the contracting officer or OIG
- The False Claims Act—how whistleblower (qui tam) lawsuits work, the treble damages exposure, and why this is the most feared statute in government contracting
- The Organizational Conflict of Interest (OCI) rules—how advising the government on requirements can disqualify you from competing for the resulting contract
- The revolving door—hiring restrictions for former government employees (18 U.S.C. § 207), cooling-off periods, and how to hire from DoD without violating the law
- Anti-kickback rules and gratuities laws—what you can and cannot give to government employees (hint: almost nothing)
- International anti-corruption: FCPA (Foreign Corrupt Practices Act) exposure for companies selling internationally through Foreign Military Sales (FMS) or Direct Commercial Sales (DCS)
- Contract administration: deliverables, progress reporting, CDRL (Contract Data Requirements List) management, and the consequences of poor performance (cure notices, show-cause notices, default termination)
- Maintaining security compliance over time—change management, continuous monitoring, annual DCSA reviews
- Mergers and acquisitions—the CFIUS (Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States) review process and why foreign acquisition of your company may be blocked
- Planning for the long game—why defense tech companies take longer to build but can be extraordinarily durable businesses once established

The reader should finish this chapter understanding that defense contracting is a long-term game that rewards companies with strong compliance cultures, and that the biggest risks come not from competitors but from regulatory violations.

## How to Use These Prompts

Each prompt above is designed to be given to a researcher or AI system to produce a comprehensive, encyclopedic chapter of approximately 800-1,200 lines. The prompts are ordered to build knowledge progressively:

1. **Chapters 1-3** establish the landscape, the players, and the Intelligence Community
2. **Chapters 4-5** cover the administrative and legal prerequisites
3. **Chapters 6-7** explain the pathways in and the financial infrastructure
4. **Chapters 8-9** address cybersecurity and classified work
5. **Chapters 10-11** cover IP protection and export controls
6. **Chapters 12-13** tackle the valley of death and Congress
7. **Chapters 14-15** address relationship-driven sales and the venture ecosystem
8. **Chapter 16** covers long-term compliance and ethics

Each chapter should include practical checklists, real-world examples, and warnings about common mistakes. The tone should be direct, no-nonsense, and written for a founder who is smart but has zero background in defense contracting. Treat the reader like an intelligent adult who needs to be told what they don't know they don't know.
